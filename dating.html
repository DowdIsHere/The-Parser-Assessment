The Dating Profiles

All 27.
One is you.

Each profile describes how that parser shows up in dating — what they bring, what they need, and what happens when they meet someone running different architecture.

Group One
Concrete
Parsers
People who trust what's real, tested, and tangible. They date with their feet on the ground — experience over theory, proof over promise.
Concrete Parsers · Group Image
01

Sharp

"I remember what you said. And what you did."

Sharp tracks the record. Every promise, every pattern, every time actions matched words or didn't. Dating them means being consistent — they're building a case file, not collecting butterflies.

02

Seasoned

"I've learned what works. I'm not guessing."

Seasoned has done the reps. They know what they need because they've tested it — in past relationships, in their own growth, in the hard lessons they actually learned. They're not cynical. They're calibrated.

03

Legacy

"I remember what matters. I hope you do too."

Legacy holds the history. The anniversaries, the stories, the way things became what they are. Dating them means honoring what came before — not just building something new.

04

Embodied

"I feel it before I can explain it."

Embodied knows in their body. The gut sense, the physical read, the something's-off before anyone's said anything wrong. Dating them requires presence — they're reading you in ways words don't cover.

05

Grounded

"I see what's actually happening."

Grounded cuts through the noise. While others spin narratives, they notice the obvious — what's working, what's not, what nobody is addressing. Dating them is refreshingly simple, if you can handle direct.

06

Attuned

"I feel how you're feeling. Right now."

Attuned reads the room before anyone speaks. The micro-expression, the shift, the energy change. Dating them means being seen — completely, continuously, in real time. It's intimate. It can also be a lot.

07

Intentional

"I know what I'm building."

Intentional is already three steps ahead. They date with a direction — not controlling, but deliberate. If you're not going somewhere together, they'll notice before you do.

08

Resilient

"I've already thought about what could go wrong."

Resilient runs scenarios. Not anxiously — practically. They want to know how you handle things before things happen. Dating them means being tested, gently, continuously. They're checking if you're solid.

09

Reliable

"My word means something."

Reliable tracks commitments. Theirs and yours. If they said they'd call, they call. If you said you'd be there, they noticed when you weren't. Trust is built in the small deliveries.

Group Two
Balanced
Parsers
People who move between concrete and abstract, adjusting to the moment. They can meet you where you are — if you give them room to shift.
Balanced Parsers · Group Image
10

Integrated

"I've done the work. I know who I am."

Integrated has synthesized their experience into self-knowledge. They're not looking for someone to complete them — they're looking for someone who's done their own work too.

11

Coherent

"I see how things connect."

Coherent holds the thread. Past to present, cause to effect, what happened to what it means. Dating them means your story matters — the whole arc, not just the highlight reel.

12

Reconciled

"I hold the history of us."

Reconciled remembers everything — not to punish, but to understand. Dating them means being tracked. The record exists because the relationship matters.

13

Centered

"I know when I'm off."

Centered has an internal anchor. They don't need you to regulate them — they can find their own ground. Dating them is stable. Sometimes too stable, if you need more reactivity.

14

Equanimous

"I'm not adding extra."

Equanimous stays calm when everyone else is spinning. They don't escalate. They don't match your chaos. Dating them is grounding — until you want them to meet you in the intensity.

15

Empathetic

"I feel you before you tell me."

Empathetic reads your emotional state continuously. They know when something's off before you've named it. Dating them means being seen — and sometimes being seen before you're ready.

16

Actualized

"I'm always becoming."

Actualized lives in the gap between who they are and who they're becoming. Growth isn't a goal — it's the medium. Dating them means keeping up, or at least not holding them back.

17

Harmonious

"I see the better way."

Harmonious spots the optimization everyone else misses. They want things to work better — including the relationship. Dating them means being improved, which is great until it feels like criticism.

18

Collaborative

"I want to build this together."

Collaborative gets excited about what you could create as a team. The relationship isn't just a connection — it's a project. Dating them means co-creating, or feeling like you're not contributing.

Group Three
Abstract
Parsers
People who see patterns, connections, and possibilities. They date in concepts and trajectories — thinking about what things mean, not just what they are.
Abstract Parsers · Group Image
19

Sentimental

"I carry what we've been through."

Sentimental holds feelings as data. The emotional texture of experiences stays vivid — not as nostalgia, but as the actual medium of memory. Dating them means every moment is being archived.

20

Reflective

"I understand after."

Reflective makes sense of things in hindsight. The meaning emerges with distance. Dating them means waiting — they'll know what they feel, just not always in the moment.

21

Idealized

"Some things shouldn't be compromised."

Idealized holds standards as sacred. Not rigid — principled. They've decided what matters, and they're not negotiating on the core. Dating them means clearing the bar, or not.

22

Introspective

"I know my own mind."

Introspective has mapped their interior. They know what they think, why they react, what patterns they're running. Dating them is deep — if you can meet them inside.

23

Mindful

"I'm here. Fully."

Mindful notices the texture of the moment. Not planning, not replaying — present. Dating them is intimate in a way that has nothing to do with intensity. It's just attention.

24

Intuitive

"I already know."

Intuitive reads the unspoken. Who's aligned, who's pretending, what's really happening between people. Dating them means being transparent — they'll see through it anyway.

25

Visionary

"I see where this is going."

Visionary is already downstream. They imagine futures before deciding on the present. Dating them means being a good investment — not in money, in trajectory.

26

Foresighted

"I see what's coming before it arrives."

Foresighted perceives trajectories. Not predictions — perception. They're often right, often early, often frustrated that no one else sees it yet. Dating them means trusting their timeline.

27

Altruistic

"I'm thinking bigger than us."

Altruistic cares about impact beyond the couple. What are you building together that matters to more than just you? Dating them means sharing a mission, or feeling like you're too small.

03
The Geometry

When Dating Parsers
Collide.

Every date that felt off for reasons you couldn't explain — this is why. It wasn't chemistry. It was geometry. Two lenses processing the same moment through completely different architectures.

Axis One

Time
Collisions

Time Collisions · Domain Image
The Core Collision
Past-Oriented × Future-Oriented
The Setup

She needs to complete what happened before she can move forward. He treats decisions as completion and allocates forward. They're running on different clocks.

The Collision

"We already talked about this" vs. "We never finished talking about it." He's confused when she brings things back. She feels like they never actually resolve.

The Way Through

Different timelines, not different values.

She flags what's still open. He asks "Is this finished for you?" before closing. They build the bridge by naming the architecture, not blaming the person.

Face-Off #2
Present × Future
The Setup

She's here, now, fully present. He's already imagining where this relationship goes in five years. She wants this moment. He wants the trajectory.

The Collision

"Can we just be here?" vs. "Where is this going?" She feels rushed. He feels like they're standing still. Both are frustrated by the other's time orientation.

The Way Through

Presence doesn't mean stagnation. Trajectory doesn't mean absence.

Give the present-oriented partner dedicated now-time without future-talk. Give the future-oriented partner space to share vision without it being pressure. Alternate, don't average.

Face-Off #3
Present × Past
The Setup

She's in the moment — what's happening right now is what's real. He's processing what happened — the context, the history, the thread.

The Collision

"Why are you still thinking about that?" vs. "Because it hasn't been finished!" She feels like he's stuck. He feels like she's dismissive.

The Way Through

Completion and presence serve different functions.

Let the past-oriented partner finish processing before expecting full presence. Let the present-oriented partner have moments that don't carry historical weight. Both are valid time modes.

Finding Synergy
When Time Differences Complete Each Other

The same time differences that cause collisions are exactly what create a full timeline. One holds the past. One holds the future. Together they have depth and direction.

What the Past Brings

Memory, continuity, the story of how you got here. The ability to learn from what happened. The record that proves this relationship matters.

What the Future Brings

Vision, direction, the sense of going somewhere. The ability to plan, to build, to imagine what you're creating together.

The Combined Effect

A relationship with roots and wings.

When both time orientations are honored, the relationship has both — history that grounds it and trajectory that propels it. The collision becomes completion.

Axis Two

Reference
Collisions

Reference Collisions · Domain Image
The Core Collision
Self-Oriented × Other-Oriented
The Setup

He processes from the inside out — his own experience, his own understanding, his own needs. She processes from the outside in — reading others, tracking relationships, feeling the space between.

The Collision

"Why are you so self-focused?" vs. "Why do you care so much what other people think?" She feels like she's doing all the relational work. He feels monitored and crowded.

The Way Through

Self-reference isn't selfishness. Other-reference isn't insecurity.

These are processing orientations, not moral failures. Invite the self-oriented partner into "us" thinking explicitly. Give the other-oriented partner space to not track everyone's state constantly.

Face-Off #2
Other × Other
The Setup

Both partners are oriented toward others — reading the room, tracking relational dynamics, attuning to what people around them need.

The Collision

"Who's taking care of us?" Neither is naturally tracking their own needs. Both might over-give, over-accommodate, or lose themselves in the relationship.

The Way Through

Two outward-facing people need to schedule inward time.

Build explicit practices for individual needs — not hoping someone will notice, but declaring them. Take turns being the "self" that gets attended to.

Face-Off #3
Self × Self
The Setup

Both partners process from internal reference — their own experience, their own needs, their own understanding. Neither naturally tracks the relational space.

The Collision

"Who's tracking us?" Two parallel individuals might feel more like roommates than partners. The relationship itself doesn't get attended to unless someone deliberately shifts.

The Way Through

Build "we" into the schedule.

If neither naturally orients outward, create structures that force relational check-ins. Regular state-of-the-union conversations. Rituals that are about us, not just me-plus-you.

Finding Synergy
When Reference Differences Balance Each Other

One partner holds the individual. One partner holds the relational. Together they have both — healthy independence and genuine connection.

What Self-Orientation Brings

Groundedness, independence, knowing one's own mind. The ability to not lose yourself in the relationship. A stable center to return to.

What Other-Orientation Brings

Attunement, care, tracking the relational field. The ability to notice when something's off. The thread that holds you together.

The Combined Effect

A relationship with both anchor and thread.

When both reference orientations are present, the relationship has stability (someone's always grounded) and connection (someone's always tracking). Neither function goes unattended.

Axis Three

Altitude
Collisions

Altitude Collisions · Domain Image
The Core Collision
Abstract × Concrete
The Setup

He thinks in patterns, principles, systems — the shape of things. She thinks in specifics, details, tangibles — the actual thing that happened.

The Collision

"You're missing the point!" vs. "You're not dealing with what actually happened!" He explains the pattern. She needs the particular acknowledged first.

The Way Through

Land the specific before you zoom out.

The abstract partner isn't avoiding the concrete — they're framing. The concrete partner isn't missing the pattern — they're grounding. Sequence matters: specific first, pattern second.

Face-Off #2
Concrete × Concrete
The Setup

Both partners live in specifics — what actually happened, what was actually said, what can actually be proven.

The Collision

The argument gets stuck in detail warfare. "You said X." "No, I said Y." No one zooms out to see the pattern or ask what it means.

The Way Through

Someone has to ask "What's this about?"

Two concrete partners may need to artificially invoke the abstract. Pause the detail dispute and ask: what pattern are we in? What's the actual issue here?

Face-Off #3
Abstract × Abstract
The Setup

Both partners think in patterns, principles, and systems. Conversations spiral into meta-discussion. Nothing lands.

The Collision

"We're talking about talking about it." Two abstract partners can theorize a relationship to death without ever dealing with the concrete reality in front of them.

The Way Through

Ground it in one specific.

Stop the meta-conversation and name one concrete instance. "What's an example of this?" forces the landing that two abstract partners will naturally avoid.

Finding Synergy
When Altitude Differences Create Depth

One partner holds the specific. One partner holds the pattern. Together they have both — accuracy and meaning.

What Concrete Brings

Precision, evidence, grounding. The ability to deal with what's actually happening. The facts that keep abstractions honest.

What Abstract Brings

Pattern recognition, meaning-making, the big picture. The ability to see what things add up to. The interpretation that gives facts significance.

The Combined Effect

A relationship that's both true and meaningful.

When both altitude orientations are present, the relationship can handle detail and derive meaning. Arguments get resolved (concrete) and understood (abstract).
