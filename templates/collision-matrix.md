## How This Document Works

This matrix defines the **rules** for what happens when two Parser Profiles interact. Every relationship report — Couples, Social, Children (parent-child), Clinicians (clinician-patient), Teams, Billets — draws from these rules.

The matrix operates on three levels:
1. **Dimension-Level Rules** — What happens when Spatial meets Spatial, Temporal meets Temporal, Reference meets Reference
2. **Cross-Dimension Interactions** — How gaps in one dimension affect the experience of gaps in another
3. **Context Weights** — Which dimensions matter most in which domains

---

## PART 1: THE THREE DIMENSIONS

### Dimension Definitions (Locked)

| Dimension | Axis | Poles | What It Governs |
|-----------|------|-------|-----------------|
| **Spatial** | Dorsal-Ventral | Concrete ↔ Abstract | How big-picture vs. detail-oriented processing runs. Concrete = specific, sensory, example-first. Abstract = principle-first, pattern-seeking, conceptual. |
| **Temporal** | Anterior-Posterior | Past ↔ Future | Where attention naturally orients in time. Past = what happened, what was learned, what's owed. Future = what's coming, what's possible, what's being built. |
| **Reference** | Medial-Lateral | Self ↔ Other | Whose experience registers first. Self = internal compass, personal meaning. Other = group impact, relational field. |

### Position Values

Each dimension has five positions:
- **0** — Definite Pole A (Concrete / Past / Self)
- **25** — Leaning Pole A
- **50** — Balanced
- **75** — Leaning Pole B
- **100** — Definite Pole B (Abstract / Future / Other)

## PART 2: DIMENSION-LEVEL COLLISION RULES

For each dimension, define what happens when Person A and Person B occupy different positions.

### 2.1 SPATIAL DIMENSION (Concrete ↔ Abstract)

#### Match Patterns

| A Position | B Position | Classification | Effect |
|------------|------------|----------------|--------|
| Same pole | Same pole | **Full Match** | Shared processing altitude. Communication flows naturally. Risk: shared blind spot (both miss what the other altitude would catch). |
| Same pole | Leaning same | **Near Match** | Minor translation needed. The leaning partner can flex. |
| Balanced | Any | **Adaptive Match** | Balanced partner can meet the other where they are. May feel like they're always translating. |
| Leaning A | Leaning B | **Mild Gap** | Different defaults, but both can flex. Friction is manageable with awareness. |
| Definite A | Definite B | **Full Opposition** | Maximum friction on this dimension. Each experiences the other as operating at wrong altitude. |

#### Spatial Collision Dynamics

**Concrete → Abstract friction:**
- Concrete feels Abstract is "floating," "ungrounded," "not dealing with reality"
- Concrete needs the specific example, the actual case, the thing that happened
- Concrete trusts what can be pointed to

**Abstract → Concrete friction:**
- Abstract feels Concrete is "stuck in details," "missing the point," "can't see the pattern"
- Abstract needs the principle, the system, the underlying structure
- Abstract trusts what can be derived

**Bridge phrase (Concrete → Abstract):**
"Here's the principle I'm seeing — let me show you the specific case that made me see it."

**Bridge phrase (Abstract → Concrete):**
"I know you need to see how this works in practice — let me connect the pattern to a real example."

#### Spatial in Relationships

- **Couples:** Spatial gaps cause "we're talking past each other" experiences. One wants to discuss the specific incident; the other wants to discuss what it means.
- **Parenting:** Parent-child spatial mismatch is high-friction in homework, instruction-giving, and explaining decisions.
- **Teams:** Spatial diversity is an asset (strategists + executors), but requires explicit translation.
- **Clinicians:** Must meet patient at their altitude. Abstract explanations to Concrete patients lose compliance.

### 2.2 TEMPORAL DIMENSION (Past ↔ Future)

#### Match Patterns

| A Position | B Position | Classification | Effect |
|------------|------------|----------------|--------|
| Same pole | Same pole | **Full Match** | Shared time orientation. Risk: no one holds the other end of time (both stuck in history or both unanchored from it). |
| Same pole | Leaning same | **Near Match** | Minor friction. The leaning partner can visit the other direction. |
| Balanced | Any | **Adaptive Match** | Balanced partner holds both directions. May feel pulled between them. |
| Leaning A | Leaning B | **Mild Gap** | Different defaults, but negotiable. "Order" friction (which comes first). |
| Definite A | Definite B | **Full Opposition** | Maximum friction. Each feels the other is ignoring essential information. |

#### Temporal Collision Dynamics

**Past → Future friction:**
- Past feels Future is "skipping the reckoning," "ignoring what happened," "building on an unhealed foundation"
- Past needs acknowledgment of what was sacrificed, what was learned, what's owed
- Past trusts what has been proven by experience

**Future → Past friction:**
- Future feels Past is "stuck," "dwelling," "refusing to move on"
- Future needs forward motion, trajectory, possibility
- Future trusts what can be built

**Bridge phrase (Past → Future):**
"I'm not trying to stay stuck — I need this acknowledged so we can actually move forward without repeating it."

**Bridge phrase (Future → Past):**
"I'm not dismissing what happened — I'm trying to see where we can go once we've honored it."

#### Temporal in Relationships

- **Couples:** Temporal is often the primary friction dimension. "Let it go" vs. "We haven't dealt with this" is a Temporal collision.
- **Parenting:** Past-oriented parent holds family history and lessons; Future-oriented parent pushes growth. Child needs both.
- **Teams:** Past holds institutional memory; Future holds strategic vision. Both are required. Gap must be explicitly bridged.
- **Clinicians:** Patient's temporal orientation shapes treatment engagement. Past-oriented patients need the "why" from history; Future-oriented patients need the "where this leads."

### 2.3 REFERENCE DIMENSION (Self ↔ Other)

#### Match Patterns

| A Position | B Position | Classification | Effect |
|------------|------------|----------------|--------|
| Same pole | Same pole | **Full Match** | Shared orientation. Risk: if both Self, no one tracks the relationship; if both Other, no one tracks cost to selves. |
| Same pole | Leaning same | **Near Match** | Minor flex needed. |
| Balanced | Any | **Adaptive Match** | Balanced partner can hold both perspectives. May feel split. |
| Leaning A | Leaning B | **Mild Gap** | Different defaults, but can be negotiated. |
| Definite A | Definite B | **Full Opposition** | Maximum friction. Each feels the other is ignoring essential concerns. |

#### Reference Collision Dynamics

**Self → Other friction:**
- Self feels Other is "losing themselves," "not knowing what they want," "unable to hold a position"
- Self needs internal coherence, personal meaning, individual clarity
- Self trusts what resonates internally

**Other → Self friction:**
- Other feels Self is "selfish," "not seeing the impact," "missing the relational field"
- Other needs group harmony, relational awareness, collective consideration
- Other trusts what serves the whole

**Bridge phrase (Self → Other):**
"I know you're thinking about everyone — help me understand how this lands for you personally."

**Bridge phrase (Other → Self):**
"I know this matters to you internally — let me show you how it connects to the people around us."

#### Reference in Relationships

- **Couples:** Reference match is the deepest bond OR the deepest blind spot. Two Other-oriented partners may both deplete themselves with no one watching the cost. Two Self-oriented partners may both feel unseen.
- **Parenting:** Other-oriented parent tracks the child and family system; Self-oriented parent models healthy boundaries. Both are needed.
- **Teams:** Reference diversity creates coverage (who's watching the people vs. who's watching the mission). Requires naming.
- **Clinicians:** Patient's reference orientation shapes what "success" means to them. Other-oriented patients may need permission to prioritize their own healing.

## PART 3: CROSS-DIMENSION INTERACTIONS

Gaps don't exist in isolation. A Temporal gap is experienced differently depending on whether Spatial and Reference match or differ.

### 3.1 Dimension Stacking Rules

| Matching Dimensions | Gapped Dimension | Experience |
|---------------------|------------------|------------|
| Spatial + Reference match | Temporal gap | "We see the world the same way but face opposite directions in time." Most workable single-dimension gap. |
| Spatial + Temporal match | Reference gap | "We agree on what and when, but not on for whom." Can feel like values clash. |
| Temporal + Reference match | Spatial gap | "We're aimed at the same thing for the same people, but at different altitudes." Translation friction, not values friction. |
| One match, two gaps | — | Moderate friction. The match provides a bridge. |
| Zero matches | Three gaps | Maximum friction. Requires deliberate bridging on all fronts. |

### 3.2 The "Opposite Corner" Rule

Maximum friction occurs when A and B are at **opposite positions on two or more dimensions**.

**Two-dimension opposition examples:**
- Concrete-Past-Self vs. Abstract-Future-Self = two dimensions opposed (Spatial + Temporal), one matched (Reference)
- Concrete-Past-Self vs. Abstract-Future-Other = three dimensions opposed (maximum friction)

**The rarity factor:**
Opposite-corner pairings are uncommon in couples and friendships (people don't usually choose them). They're common in:
- Workplaces (selection isn't based on Parser fit)
- Families (genetics doesn't guarantee Parser match)
- Clinician-patient relationships (patient doesn't choose based on Parser)

### 3.3 The "Same Corner" Rule

When A and B share the same position on all three dimensions, they experience:
- Deep, immediate understanding ("finally someone who gets it")
- Shared blind spots with no internal correction
- Risk of echo chamber / mutual reinforcement of weaknesses
- May need external input to see what they both miss

## PART 4: CONTEXT WEIGHTS

Different domains weight the three dimensions differently.

### 4.1 Couples Context

| Dimension | Weight | Why |
|-----------|--------|-----|
| **Reference** | Highest | Who you're each looking out for determines whether the relationship feels like "us" or "you and me." |
| **Temporal** | High | Past vs. Future orientation drives the most common recurring fights. |
| **Spatial** | Moderate | Communication friction, but less existential than Reference/Temporal. |

**Primary collision pattern in Couples:** Temporal opposition with Reference match ("we both care about the same people but face opposite directions in time").

### 4.2 Parenting Context

| Dimension | Weight | Why |
|-----------|--------|-----|
| **Spatial** | Highest | Parent-child Spatial mismatch creates learning/communication friction daily. |
| **Temporal** | High | Parent's time orientation shapes whether they push forward or hold history. |
| **Reference** | Moderate | Parent's Reference shapes whether they track the child vs. the family system vs. themselves. |

**Primary collision pattern in Parenting:** Spatial gap between parent and child ("I can't learn the way you're teaching").

### 4.3 Teams Context

| Dimension | Weight | Why |
|-----------|--------|-----|
| **Temporal** | Highest | Teams need both Past (institutional memory) and Future (strategic vision). |
| **Reference** | High | Self-oriented members hold individual accountability; Other-oriented hold team cohesion. |
| **Spatial** | High | Concrete executes; Abstract strategizes. Both required. |

**Primary collision pattern in Teams:** Temporal collision ("we need to move vs. we need to learn from history") and Spatial collision ("just tell me what to do vs. explain the strategy").

### 4.4 Clinician Context

| Dimension | Weight | Why |
|-----------|--------|-----|
| **Spatial** | Highest | Patient's Spatial position determines whether abstract explanations land or need translation. |
| **Temporal** | High | Patient's time orientation shapes treatment engagement and motivation framing. |
| **Reference** | Moderate | Patient's Reference position shapes what "success" means to them and who they're healing for. |

**Primary collision pattern in Clinician-Patient:** Spatial mismatch (clinician speaks Abstract, patient needs Concrete).

### 4.5 Billets Context

| Dimension | Weight | Depends on Role |
|-----------|--------|-----------------|
| **Spatial** | Variable | Strategy roles weight Abstract; Execution roles weight Concrete. |
| **Temporal** | Variable | Innovation roles weight Future; Compliance/QA roles weight Past. |
| **Reference** | Variable | Leadership roles may weight either; customer-facing often weights Other. |

**Primary analysis in Billets:** Role-specific ideal profile vs. candidate's actual profile. Gap = development need or poor fit.

### 4.6 Social Context

| Dimension | Weight | Why |
|-----------|--------|-----|
| **Reference** | Highest | Friendships live or die on whether both people feel seen. |
| **Spatial** | Moderate | Affects conversation quality but less existential than couples. |
| **Temporal** | Lower | Friends don't share a timeline the way couples do. |

**Primary collision pattern in Social:** Reference mismatch ("I need to process this with you vs. I need to process this alone").

## PART 5: REPORT GENERATION LOGIC

### 5.1 Section Generation Rules

Every relationship report contains these sections, generated from the collision rules above:

| Section | Source Logic |
|---------|--------------|
| **The Shape of the Two of You** | Identify matches (≤1 position apart) and gaps (≥2 positions apart) on each dimension. Lead with the bond (matches), then the gaps. |
| **Three Plain Differences** | For each gapped dimension, generate the friction description using the collision dynamics above. Order by context weight (highest first). |
| **The Core of It** | Synthesize the pattern: what's actually happening underneath the friction. Use the cross-dimension interaction rules. |
| **Where It Works** | For each match, describe the synergy. For each gap, describe the complementary strength (what A brings that B lacks, and vice versa). |
| **Where It Breaks** | For each gap, describe the failure mode. Use the specific collision dynamics for that dimension. |
| **What You Each Mean / What the Other Hears** | Generate 2-3 misinterpretation pairs per gapped dimension using the friction dynamics. |
| **What Each of You Can Do** | Generate bridge phrases and behavioral recommendations for each gapped dimension. |
| **Domain-Specific Sections** | Parenting, Money, Closeness, Future — apply context weights and generate from the relevant dimension rules. |
| **The One Thing to Remember** | Synthesize into one sentence: the core dynamic + the core risk + the core recommendation. |


### 5.3 Prose Generation Guidelines

- **Never use dimension language in client-facing prose.** No "your Temporal orientation" — instead, "the direction you face in time."
- **Never expose coordinates or scores.** All output is behavioral description.
- **Lead with bond, then gap.** The reader should feel understood before being challenged.
- **Use "both right" framing for gaps.** Neither position is wrong; they're reading different chapters of the same situation.
- **End every friction section with a bridge.** Never leave a gap without a way across.

## PART 6: PRIORITY PAIRINGS

### 6.1 High-Frequency Couples Pairings

Based on compatibility research and relationship counseling patterns, these pairings occur frequently and should have hand-crafted content:

**Same-Dimension Opposition (most common friction source):**
1. Visionary × Sentimental (Abstract-Future-Self × Abstract-Past-Self) — Temporal opposition only

3. Altruistic × Reconciled (Abstract-Future-Other × Balanced-Past-Other) — Temporal opposition, mild Spatial gap ✓ DONE
4. Intentional × Seasoned (Concrete-Future-Self × Concrete-Past-Balanced) — Temporal opposition, mild Reference gap
5. Reliable × Legacy (Concrete-Future-Other × Concrete-Past-Other) — Temporal opposition only

**High-Compatibility Pairings (marketing/demo value):**
6. Visionary × Visionary — Same corner, deep bond, shared blind spot
7. Grounded × Grounded — Same corner, practical alignment
8. Empathetic × Empathetic — Same corner, relational harmony

**Maximum Friction Pairings (clinical/counseling value):**
9. Visionary × Legacy (Abstract-Future-Self × Concrete-Past-Other) — Three-dimension opposition
10. Introspective × Reliable (Abstract-Present-Self × Concrete-Future-Other) — Three-dimension opposition

Based on workplace friction patterns:
