The 27
Kid Parsers.

Every kid takes in the world a little differently — not in what they like or believe, but in what they notice first. That first notice is their lens. Here are all 27.

Kid Parsers · Hero Image

Think of it like a camera. Some kids use a close-up lens — they see what's right here, in detail. Some use a wide-angle lens — they see the whole big picture. And some have an adjustable lens that zooms in and out. None is better. They're just different ways of seeing.

01
The Kid Parser Profiles

Grouped by how they
take things in.

There's no better or worse one to be — it's just the angle your kid is standing at. Once you can see theirs, a lot of everyday friction starts to make sense.

Group One
Close-Up
Lenses
Kids who trust what's right in front of them — what they've done, what they can see, and what they know from real experience.
Close-Up Lenses · Group Image
01

Sharp

aka The Expert
The kid who remembers what really happened.

This kid trusts what they've lived through. When someone gives advice, they're already checking it against their own experience — what worked, what didn't, and exactly why. They remember the specifics. That precision isn't stubbornness — it's earned.

02

Seasoned

aka The Craftsperson
The kid who's figured out how to do it right.

This kid has done the reps. While others are still guessing, they've tested and refined until they know what holds up. Expertise for them isn't a grade — it's accumulated proof. They're the one who remembers why something works, long after everyone else just accepts it.

03

Legacy

aka The Caretaker
The kid who remembers and keeps the story.

Someone has to remember. This kid holds the names, the contributions, the specific moments when things changed — the shared history others forget too easily. This isn't nostalgia; it's stewardship. They understand that what exists now was built by people, and honoring that matters.

04

Embodied

aka The Maker
The kid who learns by doing and feels things in their gut.

This kid's body knows before their mind catches up. They register the subtle signal — the tension, the shift, the "something's off" — before anyone else sees it. Reality for them isn't abstract; it's what they're physically experiencing right now.

05

Grounded

aka The Observer
The kid who sees what's actually there.

This kid sees through the noise. While others complicate, they notice what's actually happening — the obvious thing no one is addressing. Simplicity isn't limitation for them; it's clarity. And yes, it can be frustrating when everyone else needs three conversations to reach what they saw in five minutes.

06

Attuned

aka The Friend
The kid who feels how everyone's feeling.

This kid reads rooms before anyone speaks. The shifted posture, the micro-expression, the energy change when tension enters — they catch it all. This isn't a skill they developed; it's just how they experience being around people.

07

Intentional

aka The Builder
The kid who's already building what others are still wishing for.

This kid is already building something that won't matter for months. While others react to what's happening, they're three steps ahead, constructing conditions for a future they've clearly pictured. Life isn't something that happens to them — it's something they're deliberately assembling.

08

Resilient

aka The Planner
The kid who's ready for what others don't see coming.

This kid runs scenarios. Not anxiously — practically. They want to know what could go wrong so they can have a plan before it does. Preparation isn't worry for them; it's care expressed in advance. When things go sideways, they're the one who already thought about this.

09

Reliable

aka The Organizer
The kid whose word you can always count on.

This kid remembers what they promised. Long after others have forgotten they asked, they're still tracking the commitment. Following through isn't a burden — it's how they show that their word means something. Trust is built in the details of delivery.

Group Two
Adjustable
Lenses
Kids who shift with the moment — past or future, themselves or others, the practical or the big picture, depending on what the situation calls for.
Adjustable Lenses · Group Image
10

Integrated

aka The Collector
The kid who really thinks about who they are.

This kid's insights come from synthesis. They learn more from reflecting on what they've lived through than from any outside source — connecting their own experiences into patterns that explain more than the individual moments ever could. Understanding, for them, is an inside job.

11

Coherent

aka The Rememberer
The kid who remembers how things got the way they are.

This kid sees the threads. Where others experience events as disconnected, they see the continuity — how now relates to then, how patterns repeat with variation. Making sense of the present requires understanding the story that led here.

12

Reconciled

aka The Nurturer
The kid who remembers what happened to everybody.

This kid witnesses what others want to move past. Group histories live in them — who contributed what, how decisions were made, what was lost along the way. Healing isn't about forgetting; it's about truly seeing what happened so that moving forward doesn't mean pretending it didn't.

13

Centered

aka The Individual
The kid who stays calm and knows where they stand.

This kid knows when they're off before anyone else notices. There's an internal anchor that doesn't depend on what's happening around them — a stable point they can return to when things get turbulent. Their presence comes from that center.

14

Equanimous

aka The Balanced Thinker
The kid who stays calm when everyone else is freaking out.

People call this kid calm. They'd say they're just not adding extra. What's happening is already happening — reacting to it doesn't change it, just creates more turbulence. This isn't detachment; it's efficiency. Most drama is optional. They've simply opted out.

15

Empathetic

aka The Connector
The kid who notices how everybody's feeling.

This kid feels the room shift before anyone names it. Emotional undercurrents register in them like weather changes — present, unmistakable, worth attending to. Their presence creates a container for what others are experiencing, sometimes before they've fully processed their own response.

16

Actualized

aka The Explorer
The kid who's always leveling up.

The person this kid is becoming feels more real than who they are today. They live in the gap between current self and possible self — and that gap energizes rather than discourages them. Growth isn't a goal; it's the medium they move through.

17

Harmonious

aka The Strategist
The kid who finds the way everybody wins.

This kid sees the optimization everyone else misses. Systems could work better — not theoretically, but obviously, practically, if anyone would just adjust the friction points. This isn't criticism; it's perception. The improvements are right there.

18

Collaborative

aka The Guide
The kid who sees what we could build together.

This kid gets excited about collective potential. What could groups accomplish if they actually aligned? The possibility energizes them — and the waste when they don't frustrates them equally. Building something together isn't just a strategy; it's the point.

Group Three
Wide-Angle
Lenses
Kids who naturally see the bigger picture — the patterns, the connections, and where things are heading, not just what's right in front of them.
Wide-Angle Lenses · Group Image
19

Sentimental

aka The Artist
The kid who remembers how things felt.

Feelings are this kid's archive. They remember the emotional texture of experiences more vividly than the facts — and those feelings aren't behind them. They're layered into how they perceive today, still coloring everything. The past isn't something they visit; it's something they carry.

20

Reflective

aka The Wise One
The kid who figures it all out afterward.

Understanding comes after for this kid. They make sense of things in hindsight — not because they're slow, but because meaning emerges from distance. The immediate moment is data; the reflection is insight. They see what something meant long after everyone else has moved on.

21

Idealized

aka The Storyteller
The kid who remembers the rules we promised.

This kid holds certain standards as sacred — not blindly, but because they see what they represent. Principles earned their place, and abandoning them isn't progress; it's forgetting. They're the keeper of what was figured out, the reminder that some things were decided for good reason.

22

Introspective

aka The Inventor
The kid who really understands their own mind.

This kid has been told they "think too much" — and wondered what others do with all that unexamined experience. Their attention turns inward naturally, mapping the territory of their own mind. This isn't overthinking; it's data collection about the instrument through which they experience everything else.

23

Mindful

aka The Philosopher
The kid who really notices what's happening right now.

This kid notices the texture of experience itself. Where others are lost in content, they perceive the quality of the moment — its particular character, this awareness, this configuration of attention. Presence isn't a practice for them; it's what they naturally do.

24

Intuitive

aka The Helper
The kid who can read the room.

This kid knows how people feel about each other before anyone has said anything. The unspoken dynamics are visible to them — who's aligned, who's in tension, who's pretending. They're just picking up on signals everyone else is sending and missing.

25

Visionary

aka The Dreamer
The kid who dreams up what could be.

This kid sees where things are heading before anyone else notices anything changing. Patterns extend forward in their mind naturally — not as worry, but as perception. They're already living in a future others haven't imagined yet.

26

Foresighted

aka The Visionary
The kid who can see what's coming next.

This kid perceives trajectories. Not predictions — something more like seeing the shape of what's emerging before it fully arrives. This isn't prophecy; it's noticing where things are headed before the trend is obvious. They're often right, which is useful. They're often early, which is frustrating.

27

Altruistic

aka The Team Captain
The kid who dreams of making things better for everyone.

This kid thinks in systems and generations. Individual outcomes matter less than collective trajectory — where are we heading, not just where am I going. They're playing a longer game. The beneficiaries of their thinking may not even be born yet.

03
Where Kids Clash

When Kid Parsers
Collide.

A lot of friction between kids isn't about personality or behavior — it's about two children processing the same moment through completely different lenses. Once you see the geometry, the fights start making sense.

Domain One

Sibling
Collisions

Sibling Collisions · Domain Image
Face-Off #1
Sharp vs. Visionary
The Setup

Sharp remembers exactly what happened last time — and has proof. Visionary is already imagining what they could build next. Both trust their own read of the situation completely.

The Collision

"You always make stuff up!" vs. "You never want to try anything new!" Sharp feels dismissed when their evidence is ignored. Visionary feels trapped when the past is used to block the future.

The Way Through

They need different roles, not the same one.

Let Sharp be the quality checker and Visionary be the idea starter. When both positions are honored, the fight over who's "right" dissolves.

Face-Off #2
Grounded vs. Sentimental
The Setup

Grounded sees what's right in front of them — the obvious, practical reality. Sentimental carries the emotional weight of everything that's happened before.

The Collision

"Why are you still upset about that?" vs. "Why don't you care about anything?" Grounded can't understand why feelings from the past keep showing up. Sentimental can't understand how you can just move on.

The Way Through

Neither is overreacting or underreacting.

Help Grounded understand that some kids carry feelings longer — it's not drama. Help Sentimental see that Grounded's calm isn't coldness — it's just a different lens.

Face-Off #3
Attuned vs. Introspective
The Setup

Attuned reads the room and knows how everyone is feeling. Introspective goes inward, mapping their own internal landscape. Both are sensitive — in opposite directions.

The Collision

"Talk to me! I can tell something's wrong!" vs. "Leave me alone, I'm thinking!" Attuned feels shut out. Introspective feels invaded. Both feel unseen.

The Way Through

One reaches out, the other reaches in — both are connecting.

Teach Attuned that silence doesn't mean rejection. Teach Introspective that check-ins aren't intrusions. Give them a signal system: "I need space" and "I'm here when you're ready."

Face-Off #4
Reliable vs. Actualized
The Setup

Reliable tracks promises and follows through on commitments. Actualized is always evolving, outgrowing agreements almost as fast as they make them.

The Collision

"You said you'd do it!" vs. "I changed my mind — I'm different now!" Reliable feels betrayed when commitments shift. Actualized feels chained when held to a version of themselves that no longer fits.

The Way Through

Growth doesn't have to break trust.

Help Actualized understand that changing plans affects other people. Help Reliable understand that growing isn't the same as breaking a promise. Build renegotiation into the family vocabulary.

Finding Synergy
The Siblings Who Cover Each Other's Blind Spots

The same differences that cause sibling fights are exactly what make siblings complete. One holds the facts; the other holds the feelings. One plans ahead; the other stays present. A family with every lens type isn't chaotic — it's covered.

Anchored · Sharp + Grounded + Reliable

These kids hold the family to what's real, what's proven, and what was promised. When things get chaotic, they're the ground under everyone's feet.

Reaching · Visionary + Sentimental + Actualized

These kids keep the family connected to what matters, what's possible, and what's worth growing toward. When things get rigid, they're the stretch.

The Combined Effect

A family that can hold its ground and still grow.

When siblings stop trying to make each other process the same way and start appreciating what each one brings, the fights don't disappear — but they start making sense. And that changes everything.

Domain Two

Friend
Collisions

Friend Collisions · Domain Image
Face-Off #1
Embodied vs. Reflective
The Setup

Embodied wants to do something — run, build, play, move. Reflective wants to sit with what just happened and figure out what it meant.

The Collision

"Come on, let's GO!" vs. "Wait, I'm still thinking about what happened." Embodied feels dragged down. Reflective feels rushed and unheard.

The Way Through

Activity and reflection aren't competing — they're taking turns.

Give them a rhythm: do something together, then talk about it after. Embodied processes through action; Reflective processes through meaning. Both are working — at different speeds.

Face-Off #2
Collaborative vs. Intentional
The Setup

Collaborative wants everyone involved — the more friends contributing, the better the project. Intentional already has a plan and knows exactly where it's going.

The Collision

"Let everyone help!" vs. "They'll mess it up — I have a system." Collaborative feels excluded. Intentional feels derailed. Both think the other is ruining the thing they care about.

The Way Through

Inclusion and vision can coexist with clear roles.

Let Intentional set the direction, let Collaborative bring in the team. "You decide what we're building; I'll help us build it together." Structure prevents the collision.

Face-Off #3
Legacy vs. Visionary
The Setup

Legacy remembers the rules, the traditions, the way things have always been done in the friend group. Visionary wants to try something completely new.

The Collision

"But we always do it this way!" vs. "That's boring — let's change it!" Legacy feels disrespected. Visionary feels suffocated. The friend group splits along the line.

The Way Through

Tradition and invention aren't enemies — they're a creative pair.

Keep the core of the tradition but let Visionary remix the edges. "We still do pizza Friday — but this time we make the pizza." Honoring what was while reaching for what could be.

Finding Synergy
The Friend Group That Holds Together

The best friend groups aren't made of kids who think the same way. They're made of kids whose different lenses complement each other — the doer, the thinker, the keeper, the dreamer, the includer, the planner.

The Glue · Collaborative + Legacy

Collaborative makes sure everyone belongs. Legacy makes sure the group remembers what makes it special. Together they create a friend group with both openness and identity.

The Spark · Visionary + Embodied

Visionary dreams up what to do next. Embodied gets everyone actually doing it. Together they keep the friend group alive and in motion.

The Combined Effect

A friend group with roots and wings.

When every kid's lens has a role, nobody gets left out and nobody gets bored. The group can remember where it's been and still try new things — because different kids are holding different pieces.

Domain Three

Classroom
Collisions

Classroom Collisions · Domain Image
Face-Off #1
Resilient vs. Empathetic
The Setup

Resilient has already thought through what could go wrong and has a plan. Empathetic feels the emotional weight of the room and is processing everyone's stress at once.

The Collision

"Just stick to the plan!" vs. "Can't you see everyone's upset?" Resilient sees the Empathetic kid as derailing the group with feelings. Empathetic sees Resilient as ignoring people who are struggling.

The Way Through

Tasks and feelings both need attention — in sequence.

Let Empathetic acknowledge the room first, then let Resilient's plan take over. "Okay, this is hard — and here's what we're going to do." When feelings are honored, plans work better.

Face-Off #2
Harmonious vs. Seasoned
The Setup

Harmonious sees a better way to organize the group project — more efficient, fairer, clearly better. Seasoned knows the way that already works and has done the reps to prove it.

The Collision

"We should change how we're doing this." vs. "It works fine — I've done it before." Harmonious feels frustrated that nobody will improve. Seasoned feels their experience is being dismissed by someone with a theory.

The Way Through

Experience and optimization aren't enemies.

Let Seasoned explain what works and why. Let Harmonious propose improvements that build on what's proven rather than replacing it. Earned expertise plus fresh perspective equals the strongest solution.

Face-Off #3
Coherent vs. Equanimous
The Setup

Coherent needs to understand why things are the way they are — the story, the context, the thread from past to present. Equanimous just wants to deal with what's in front of them without the backstory.

The Collision

"But you have to understand WHY!" vs. "Who cares why? Let's just handle it." Coherent feels the other kid is being shallow. Equanimous feels Coherent is overthinking everything and slowing the group down.

The Way Through

Context and action serve different phases of the same work.

Give Coherent space to explain the background first — it takes less time than they think. Then let Equanimous drive the execution. Understanding why before acting prevents mistakes; acting without overanalyzing prevents paralysis.

Finding Synergy
The Classroom That Works for Everyone

The best classrooms don't run on a single mode — they rotate between feeling, planning, doing, and understanding. When different lenses are recognized instead of corrected, the whole room gets smarter.

The Compass · Empathetic + Coherent

Empathetic keeps the group human. Coherent keeps the group oriented. Together they make sure no one's feelings get steamrolled and no one loses the thread.

The Engine · Resilient + Harmonious + Seasoned

Resilient has the contingency plan. Harmonious sees the optimization. Seasoned knows the proven method. Together they get things done — well, efficiently, and with a backup plan.

The Combined Effect

A classroom where every lens has a job.

When kids stop being told to "pay attention the right way" and start being given roles that fit their actual lens, group work transforms from friction into flow.

Domain Four

Family
Collisions

Family Collisions · Domain Image
Axis Mismatch #1
Self-Oriented Kid · Other-Oriented Parent
The Setup

The child processes experience through their own internal reference — what they think, what they've lived, what makes sense to them. The parent's lens is tuned outward — to the group, the family, the social fabric.

The Collision

"Why are you so selfish?" — but the child isn't selfish. They're self-referential. They're building their understanding from the inside out. The parent reads independence as disconnection.

The Way Through

Self-reference isn't selfishness.

This child still cares — they just process caring differently. Invite them in rather than demanding they arrive pre-connected. "Tell me what you think" works better than "Think about others."

Axis Mismatch #2
Other-Oriented Kid · Self-Oriented Parent
The Setup

The child is tuned to what everyone around them is feeling and needing. The parent processes through their own experience and expects the child to do the same.

The Collision

"Stop worrying about everyone else" — but this child can't not notice. Their lens is pointed outward. The parent reads attunement as anxiety or people-pleasing.

The Way Through

Attunement isn't weakness.

This child's radar for others is a genuine cognitive strength. Help them learn boundaries without telling them their wiring is wrong. "You noticed that — what do you want to do about it?" honors the signal without drowning in it.

Axis Mismatch #3
Future-Oriented Kid · Past-Oriented Parent
The Setup

The child lives three steps ahead — always imagining, projecting, building toward what's next. The parent processes through what they've already experienced, what's been tested, what's been earned.

The Collision

"You need to appreciate what you have!" vs. a child who can't stop reaching for what's next. The parent feels the child is ungrateful. The child feels the parent is holding them back.

The Way Through

Forward-reaching isn't ingratitude.

This child's default is anticipation, not dissatisfaction. Connect future goals to past lessons: "You're right that there's more ahead — and here's what you've already learned that will get you there."

Axis Mismatch #4
Past-Oriented Kid · Future-Oriented Parent
The Setup

The child holds on to what's happened — traditions, memories, the way things have been. The parent is constantly pushing forward, planning, optimizing for what's coming.

The Collision

"Let it go — move forward!" vs. a child who needs to hold what matters. The parent sees a child stuck in the past. The child feels like their memories are being erased.

The Way Through

Honoring the past isn't being stuck.

This child's relationship with what's happened is how they make sense of the world. Don't rush them forward — walk them there. "That mattered. And here's where we go from here."

Axis Mismatch #5
Concrete Kid · Abstract Parent
The Setup

The child needs to see it, touch it, try it. The parent thinks in concepts, patterns, and big-picture frameworks.

The Collision

"You're not getting the point!" — but the child needs the point to arrive through experience, not explanation. Abstract advice lands as noise. The parent feels the child is being intentionally obtuse.

The Way Through

Concrete isn't slow — it's grounded.

Translate the concept into an example. Show, don't explain. "Watch this" teaches this child more than "Think about it." The understanding comes after the experience, not before.

Axis Mismatch #6
Abstract Kid · Concrete Parent
The Setup

The child is already in the meta-level — seeing patterns, asking why, connecting things that seem unrelated. The parent deals in what's practical, immediate, and proven.

The Collision

"Stop overcomplicating everything!" — but the child isn't overcomplicating. They're seeing at a different altitude. The parent feels exhausted by conversations that never seem to land. The child feels flattened.

The Way Through

Abstract isn't impractical — it's a different floor of the building.

Ask the child to walk you down from their idea to a concrete example. "That's interesting — show me what that would look like." Bridges the altitude gap without dismissing either floor.

The Six Big Mismatches Parent–Child Axis Collisions at a Glance

Every parent–child collision above maps to one of six fundamental mismatches. These aren't personality conflicts — they're geometric: two lenses pointed at different parts of the same reality.

SpatialSelf-focused or overly sensitive to others"I'm just noticing what I notice — why is that wrong?"
Temporal (Future)Ungrateful, never satisfied"I'm excited about what's next — why does that upset you?"
Temporal (Past)Stuck, resistant to change"I'm holding what matters — why do you want me to forget?"

ProcessingOverthinking or too simplistic"My mind works at this level — I can't just switch floors."
ExpressionToo quiet or too dramatic"This is how my feelings come out — it's not a performance."
The Main Thing to Remember

Your child's lens isn't a problem to fix — it's a position to understand. The friction between your parser and theirs isn't a sign that something is wrong. It's a sign that you're looking at the same world from two different angles. Once you see the geometry, the frustration turns into navigation.
