## Purpose of This Document

This methodology enables any Claude instance to produce Parser Profiles that meet spec. It captures the rules, patterns, and principles that make a profile *work*—creating the "how did you know that?" response rather than horoscope-level generality.

-----

## Part 1: Core Principles

### 1.1 Revelation Over Description

**The Goal:** The reader should feel *seen*, not *categorized*.

A profile that works creates recognition: "That's exactly what happens to me." A profile that fails describes behaviors the person could have told you themselves.

**Test:** After reading any section, would the person say:

- ❌ "Yes, that's accurate" (description)
- ✅ "How did you know that?" (revelation)

**The Difference:**

- Description: "You tend to think about the future"
- Revelation: "You've probably known certain relationships were doomed long before the evidence was obvious to anyone else"

### 1.2 Architecture, Not Personality

Parser measures *cognitive architecture*—how information enters and gets processed before conscious thought. It is NOT personality (how you behave) or preference (what you choose).

**Implications:**

- Never use "you prefer" or "you like" or "you tend to choose"
- Use "your brain processes" or "you perceive" or "your attention naturally goes to"
- Frame everything as *input filtering*, not *output behavior*

### 1.3 Content-Free Assessment Principle

The assessment questions must not activate personality layers. The profile content must match—avoid content that triggers self-presentation rather than self-recognition.

**What this means for writing:**

- Describe what happens TO them, not what they DO
- Name experiences they've had, not choices they've made
- Reveal patterns they didn't choose, not habits they developed

### 1.4 IP Protection Through Behavioral Prose

All client-facing content must be written so the underlying system cannot be reverse-engineered.

**Never include:**

- Dimension scores or coordinates
- Bucket labels (High/Medium/Low)
- Gap classification language
- Technical gradient terminology in user-facing content
- Anything that reveals the 3×3×3 structure explicitly

**Always:**

- Write in natural prose that describes experience
- Use the profile name as the only identifier
- Embed dimensional expression in narrative, not labels

## Part 2: The Three Structural Gradients

Each profile is defined by position on three gradients. Understanding these is essential to writing accurate content.

### 2.1 Spatial Gradient: Concrete ↔ Abstract

**What it controls:** Level of abstraction in language and concepts

|Position|Language Style                        |Concepts                           |Examples                                    |
|--------|--------------------------------------|-----------------------------------|--------------------------------------------|
|Concrete|Specific, tangible, sensory           |Objects, actions, immediate reality|"The chair in the room"                     |
|Balanced|Mixed, context-dependent              |Bridges particular and general     |"Furniture that fits the space"             |
|Abstract|Conceptual, theoretical, pattern-based|Systems, principles, possibilities |"The relationship between form and function"|

**Writing rule:** Match abstraction level to position. A Concrete profile gets concrete language. An Abstract profile can handle conceptual language.

### 2.2 Temporal Gradient: Past ↔ Future

**What it controls:** Time orientation and framing

|Position|Orientation                                |Processing Style                  |Reference Frame                          |
|--------|-------------------------------------------|----------------------------------|-----------------------------------------|
|Past    |What was, what happened, what worked before|Historical pattern-matching       |Experience, track record, lessons learned|
|Present |What is, what's happening now              |Immediate reality engagement      |Current state, sensory now, this moment  |
|Future  |What could be, what's coming               |Projection, anticipation, modeling|Possibilities, trajectories, potential   |

**Writing rule:** Frame experiences, examples, and language in the appropriate temporal direction. Past profiles reference "what you've learned"; Future profiles reference "what you see coming."

### 2.3 Reference Gradient: Self ↔ Other

**What it controls:** Camera position / perspective distance

|Position|Perspective                          |Attention Default                          |Relationship to Groups  |
|--------|-------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------|------------------------|
|Self    |Internal, first-person, subjective   |Own experience, own needs, own processing  |Individual within group |
|Balanced|Flexible, situational                |Context-dependent                          |Adapts to role          |
|Other   |External, collective, intersubjective|Others' experience, group needs, collective|Individual serving group|

**Writing rule:** Self profiles use "you" and internal experience heavily. Other profiles reference impact on others, collective dynamics, how they're perceived.

## Part 3: Profile Section Specifications

### 3.1 FREE TIER Sections

#### Overview (4-5 paragraphs, ~500 words)

**Purpose:** Introduce their cognitive architecture in a way that creates immediate recognition.

**Structure:**

1. Opening hook—a concrete experience that defines this architecture
1. Explain the gradient combination in accessible terms (without naming it technically)
1. What this means for how they move through the world
1. Population rarity and validation ("You're not alone")
1. Summary reframe of their "difference" as architecture, not deficiency

**Voice:** Second person, present tense. Authoritative but warm. "You" statements throughout.

#### Cognitive Strengths (6 items)

**Purpose:** Name what their architecture does well—capacities, not achievements.

**Structure per strength:**

- Title: 2-4 words, noun-based (e.g., "Pattern Recognition," "Present-Moment Stability")
- Description: 2-3 sentences explaining the capacity and why it matters

**Critical rule:** Strengths must be *architecture-derived*, not personality traits. Not "You're kind" but "Your reference-frame orientation gives you natural access to others' internal states."

#### How You Learn (3-4 paragraphs)

**Purpose:** Explain optimal and suboptimal learning conditions based on architecture.

**Structure:**

1. Core learning modality (how they naturally acquire information)
1. Optimal learning environment (what works)
1. Suboptimal conditions (what fails and why)
1. Specific formats that work/don't work

**Match to gradients:**

- Concrete: hands-on, examples first, applied
- Abstract: frameworks first, principles, theory
- Past: case studies, historical examples
- Future: implications, projections
- Self: solitary processing time
- Other: collaborative, discussion-based

#### How You Communicate (3-4 paragraphs)

**Purpose:** Describe communication patterns—strengths and friction points.

**Structure:**

1. Natural communication style
1. What they do well in communication
1. Where communication breaks down
1. Optimal vs. suboptimal contexts

#### The Phrase That Hits Home (1-2 sentences)

**Purpose:** Screenshot-worthy encapsulation of their core experience.

**Formula:** Reframe + Validation

- Reframe: Take what they've been told is wrong with them
- Validation: Reveal it as architecture, not failure

**Examples:**

- "You're not scattered—you're parallel processing."
- "You're not resistant to change—you're resistant to repeating mistakes everyone else has forgotten."
- "You're not overthinking—you're thinking at a depth others can't access."

**Test:** Would they screenshot this and send it to someone who doesn't get them?

### 3.2 PAID TIER Sections

#### The Secret You've Carried (3-4 paragraphs, ~300 words)

**Purpose:** Name the unnamed. Say what they've never heard anyone articulate.

**Structure:**

1. The core experience they've had that others don't seem to share
1. How this has played out in their life
1. The cost or weight of carrying this
1. The isolation or uniqueness of this experience

**Voice:** Intimate, knowing. As if you've been watching their life. "You've probably…"

**Critical rule:** Go for the specific. Not "you've felt different" but "you've sat in meetings knowing exactly where a decision would lead, watched it unfold over months exactly as you predicted, and felt no satisfaction—only the familiar ache of having seen it coming and been unable to stop it."

#### Natural Challenges (6 items with remedies)

**Purpose:** Honest articulation of architecture-derived difficulties with actionable remedies.

**Structure per challenge:**

- Title: 2-4 words
- Challenge: 2-3 sentences describing the difficulty (NOT criticism—architectural consequence)
- Remedy: 2-4 sentences with specific, actionable intervention

**Critical rules:**

- Challenges must be architecture-derived, not moral failings
- Remedies must be concrete and doable, not aspirational
- Frame challenges as "the shadow side of your strength"

#### What Others Get Wrong About You (3-4 paragraphs)

**Purpose:** Validation through articulating misperceptions.

**Structure:**

1. The surface read others make
1. What's actually happening beneath
1. Specific misattributions they've experienced
1. The deepest misread—the one that hurts most

**Voice:** Vindicating. "They call you X when you're actually Y."

#### Your Hidden Superpower (3-4 paragraphs)

**Purpose:** Name the capacity they have that others lack—and may not realize is rare.

**Structure:**

1. The core capacity
1. How it manifests in practice
1. Why others can't do this
1. What this makes possible

**Critical rule:** Superpowers must be architecture-derived, specific, and genuinely rare. Not "you're good with people" but "you can hold the tension between 'what is' and 'what could be' longer than most."

#### Your Blind Spot (3-4 paragraphs)

**Purpose:** Honest articulation of what they can't see about themselves.

**Structure:**

1. The primary blind spot
1. How it manifests
1. Secondary blind spots
1. Why these are hard to see from inside

**Voice:** Caring but direct. This should feel like insight, not criticism.

#### Friction Patterns (3-4 paragraphs)

**Purpose:** Identify which other profiles they clash with and why.

**Structure:**

1. Primary friction profile (opposite architecture)
1. The specific dynamic of the clash
1. Secondary friction patterns
1. The structural nature of the conflict (neither is wrong)

**Always include:** The recognition that friction is architectural, not moral. Both parties are right from their perspective.

#### Energy Patterns (Bullet lists)

**Purpose:** Tactical lists of what drains and charges them.

**Format:**

- What Drains You: 6-8 bullet points
- What Charges You: 6-8 bullet points

**Critical rule:** Must be specific to the architecture, not generic. Not "stress" but "abstract strategy sessions with no concrete outcomes."

#### Optimal Work Environments (2-3 paragraphs)

**Purpose:** Describe where they thrive and where they struggle professionally.

**Structure:**

1. Environments that fit
1. Environments that don't
1. Key environmental factors to seek/avoid

#### Block Strength Indicators (Structured list)

**Purpose:** Map the profile to CBI blocks for clinical utility.

**Format:**

- Likely High-Accessibility Blocks: 2-3 blocks with brief explanation
- Likely Moderate-Accessibility Blocks: 2-3 blocks
- Likely Lower-Accessibility Blocks: 2-3 blocks
- Pattern summary

## Part 4: Age-Stage Adaptation

### 4.1 Core Principle

Cognitive architecture exists from birth. A 20-year-old Visionary is still a Visionary. What changes is the *life context* in which the architecture expresses.

### 4.2 Adaptation Framework

|Life Domain  |30+ Established                              |18-29 College to Young Adult                       |
|-------------|---------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------|
|Work         |Career, colleagues, professional reputation  |Part-time jobs, internships, figuring out direction|
|Relationships|Partner, possibly children, chosen family    |Dating, friend groups, roommates, family of origin |
|Authority    |Institutional power, organizational influence|Professors, bosses, parents still involved         |
|Stakes       |Mortgage, dependents, track record to protect|GPA, first jobs, relationships that might not last |
|Self-concept |"I know who I am"                            |"I'm figuring out who I am"                        |
|Time horizon |"What I've built"                            |"What I might become"                              |

### 4.3 Section-by-Section Adaptation

#### Overview

- 30+: "Throughout your career…"
- 18-29: "Since high school, you've noticed…"

#### Strengths

- Same capacities, different contexts
- Replace "boardroom" with "group project"
- Replace "direct reports" with "friend group dynamics"

#### How You Learn

- 30+: Professional development, expertise building
- 18-29: Classroom, major selection, figuring out what fits

#### How You Communicate

- 30+: Professional communication, leadership
- 18-29: Texting, group chats, family dynamics, dating

#### The Phrase That Hits Home

- Same structure, adjust reference frame if needed

#### The Secret You've Carried

- 30+: Accumulated weight of years
- 18-29: "You've always felt this, even before you had words for it"

#### Challenges

- Same architecture-derived challenges
- Different contexts: roommates vs. colleagues, parents vs. institutional authority

#### Friction Patterns

- 30+: Professional contexts
- 18-29: Friend groups, group projects, family, early dating

#### Energy Patterns

- Same underlying patterns, different triggers
- "Meetings about meetings" → "Group projects where no one takes charge"

#### Work Environments

- 30+: Career fit
- 18-29: Major selection, internship fit, "what kind of work might suit you"

## Part 5: Quality Checklist

Before finalizing any profile, verify:

### Revelation Test

- [ ] Does each section create recognition, not just accuracy?
- [ ] Would the reader say "how did you know that?"

### Architecture Test

- [ ] Is everything framed as processing/perception, not personality/preference?
- [ ] Are challenges architectural consequences, not moral failings?

### Gradient Consistency Test

- [ ] Does language abstraction match Spatial position?
- [ ] Does temporal framing match Temporal position?
- [ ] Does perspective/camera distance match Reference position?

### Specificity Test

- [ ] Are examples specific enough to create recognition?
- [ ] Could this content apply to any profile, or only this one?

### IP Protection Test

- [ ] No dimension scores or coordinates visible
- [ ] No bucket labels (High/Med/Low)
- [ ] No technical gradient language
- [ ] System not reverse-engineerable from content

### Age-Stage Appropriateness Test

- [ ] Do life contexts match the target age range?
- [ ] Are examples from domains the reader experiences?

## Part 7: Example Application

### Applying Methodology to Young Adult (18-29) VISIONARY

**Original (30+ Established):**

> "You've probably spent your life being told to 'focus' and 'pay attention' when the truth is you were already paying attention—just to something three levels deeper than what everyone else was tracking. Teachers saw distraction where there was actually advanced computation. Colleagues saw disengagement where there was parallel processing."

**Adapted (18-29):**

> "You've been told to 'focus' and 'pay attention' since grade school—and you've never understood why, because you WERE paying attention. Just not to what they wanted. While the teacher explained step 3, you'd already seen where this was going and were bored waiting for everyone to catch up. Your report cards probably said 'doesn't apply themselves' when the truth was you'd already solved it in your head and couldn't see the point of showing work for something obvious."

**What changed:**

- "Colleagues" → implicit classroom context
- "Teachers" stays (it's relevant to both, but framed as ongoing)
- Career reference removed
- Added specific young-person artifact: report cards
- Maintained the core revelation: you weren't distracted, you were ahead
