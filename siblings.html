02
The Geometry

The fights that
keep happening.

Every sibling conflict maps to one of three axes. The same argument over and over isn't a behavior problem — it's a structural collision that hasn't been named.

Axis One

Time
Collisions

Time Collisions · Domain Image
Face-Off #1
Past × Future
The Setup

One sibling remembers everything — who said what, who started it, what happened last time. The other is already imagining what they could build next. They're in completely different time zones.

The Collision

"You always make stuff up!" vs. "You never want to try anything new!" Past feels dismissed. Future feels trapped. Both think the other is avoiding reality.

The Way Through

Give them different jobs.

Past is the quality checker — "Did this work before?" Future is the idea starter — "What could we try?" When both roles are honored, the fight over who's right dissolves.

Face-Off #2
Past × Present
The Setup

One sibling is still processing what happened yesterday, last week, last year. The other lives completely in the now — what's happening right in front of them is all that exists.

The Collision

"Why are you still upset about that?" vs. "Why don't you care about anything?" Present can't understand why old things keep coming back. Past can't understand how you just move on.

The Way Through

Neither is overreacting or underreacting.

Past needs completion before release. Present processes in the moment and lets go. Help Present understand that some kids carry things longer. Help Past see that Present's moving on isn't coldness.

Face-Off #3
Future × Present
The Setup

One sibling is planning three steps ahead — what happens next, what they're building toward. The other is fully here, doing what they're doing right now, not thinking about later.

The Collision

"We need a plan!" vs. "Just do it!" Future gets frustrated that Present won't think ahead. Present gets frustrated that Future won't start anything until it's perfect.

The Way Through

Plan first, then do. Or do first, then plan.

Build a sequence they both accept. Future gets planning time. Present gets action time. Neither has to abandon their orientation — they just take turns.

Finding Synergy
When Time Differences Complete Each Other

The same time differences that cause fights are exactly what give a sibling pair a complete timeline. One holds where they've been. One holds where they're going. One holds right now.

What Past Brings

Memory, continuity, learning from experience. The record of what worked and what didn't. The keeper of family stories.

What Future Brings

Vision, direction, imagination. The ability to see what's coming and plan for it. The dreamer who pulls everyone forward.

The Combined Effect

A sibling pair with both roots and wings.

When all three time orientations are represented, the siblings together have complete coverage. Someone remembers. Someone dreams. Someone acts. The family has history, presence, and direction.

Axis Two

Reference
Collisions

Reference Collisions · Domain Image
Face-Off #1
Self × Other
The Setup

One sibling processes from the inside out — their own experience, their own needs, their own understanding. The other tracks everyone else first — reading the room, feeling the group, noticing what's needed.

The Collision

"You only think about yourself!" vs. "You're so obsessed with what everyone thinks!" Self feels monitored. Other feels like they're doing all the relational work.

The Way Through

Self-reference isn't selfishness. Other-reference isn't anxiety.

Help Self understand that Other's attunement is real perception, not performance. Help Other see that Self's internal focus isn't rejection — it's just where their attention goes first.

Face-Off #2
Other × Other
The Setup

Both siblings are tuned outward — tracking what everyone needs, how people are feeling, what the group wants. Neither naturally tracks their own needs first.

The Collision

"You should have said something!" Both over-accommodate. Both lose themselves. Neither admits what they actually want until it's too late.

The Way Through

Two outward-facing kids need permission to be selfish.

Build explicit practices where each states what they want — not what's fair, not what's nice, what they actually want. Take turns. Make it required.

Face-Off #3
Self × Self
The Setup

Both siblings process from internal reference. Both know what they want. Neither naturally tracks how their choices affect the other.

The Collision

Two parallel kids, both clear on their own position, neither checking if the other is okay. "Why didn't you ask?" "Why didn't you say something?"

The Way Through

Build the check-in into the system.

Neither will naturally ask. Create rituals that require it. "Before we decide, each person says what they want." Structure replaces instinct.

Finding Synergy
When Reference Differences Balance Each Other

One sibling holds the individual. One sibling holds the relational. Together they have both — healthy independence and genuine connection.

What Self Brings

Clarity, boundaries, knowing one's own mind. The ability to say no. The anchor that keeps someone from disappearing into others.

What Other Brings

Attunement, care, social awareness. The ability to notice when something's wrong. The thread that holds relationships together.

The Combined Effect

A sibling pair with both anchor and thread.

Self-oriented siblings keep the family from losing individuals. Other-oriented siblings keep the family from fragmenting. Together they build a system that protects both autonomy and connection.

Axis Three

Altitude
Collisions

Altitude Collisions · Domain Image
Face-Off #1
Abstract × Concrete
The Setup

One sibling sees the big picture — patterns, principles, what things mean. The other sees the specific — what actually happened, what was actually said, what's actually in front of them.

The Collision

"You're missing the point!" vs. "You're not listening to what happened!" Abstract explains the pattern. Concrete needs the particular acknowledged first. Neither feels heard.

The Way Through

Specific first, then pattern.

Abstract isn't avoiding the concrete — they're framing. Concrete isn't missing the pattern — they're grounding. Land the specific thing before zooming out. Sequence matters.

Face-Off #2
Abstract × Abstract
The Setup

Both siblings think in patterns, systems, and big ideas. Conversations spiral into meta-discussion. They're talking about talking about it.

The Collision

Nothing lands. Both are theorizing. Neither gets practical. The argument keeps escalating because no one is naming the specific thing that actually happened.

The Way Through

Force the landing.

"Give me one specific example." Two abstract kids need an external prompt to get concrete. Build the question into family practice: "What's one specific thing?"

Face-Off #3
Concrete × Concrete
The Setup

Both siblings deal in specifics. What exactly was said. What exactly happened. Who did what when. No one is tracking the pattern.

The Collision

Detail warfare. "You said X." "No, I said Y." The argument gets stuck in micro-facts while the real issue — the pattern, the meaning, the why — goes unnamed.

The Way Through

Someone has to ask "What's this really about?"

Two concrete kids may need a parent to invoke the abstract. "Pause. Forget the exact words. What's the actual issue here?" Lift them out of the details.

Finding Synergy
When Altitude Differences Create Depth

One sibling holds the specific. One sibling holds the pattern. Together they have both — accuracy and meaning.

What Concrete Brings

Precision, evidence, grounding. The ability to deal with what's actually happening. The facts that keep abstract thinking honest.

What Abstract Brings

Pattern recognition, meaning-making, the big picture. The ability to see what things add up to. The interpretation that gives facts significance.

The Combined Effect

A sibling pair that's both true and meaningful.

Concrete keeps the family honest about what's happening. Abstract helps the family understand what it means. Together they build a house where both facts and interpretations matter.
