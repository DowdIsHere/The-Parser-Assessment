02
The Geometry

Where families
collide.

The fights that keep happening aren't about who's right. They're about lenses that process the same moment completely differently.

Family Pattern One

Parent ×
Parent

Parent × Parent · Domain Image
Face-Off #1
Future × Past
The Setup

One parent is already planning the college fund, the summer schedule, the five-year move. The other is still processing what happened at breakfast, last week, the last holiday.

The Collision

"We need to decide about next year" vs. "We haven't even finished talking about what happened." Future feels blocked. Past feels rushed. Both think the other isn't taking parenting seriously.

The Way Through

Different time horizons, same commitment.

Past needs completion before moving forward. Future needs movement to feel progress. Build the sequence: close the open loop, then plan. Neither orientation is avoidance.

Face-Off #2
Abstract × Concrete
The Setup

One parent thinks in principles: "We need to teach them responsibility." The other thinks in specifics: "Did anyone pack their lunch? Did they brush their teeth?"

The Collision

"You're missing the bigger picture" vs. "You're not handling the actual details." Both feel like they're doing the harder work. Both feel like the other isn't pulling their weight.

The Way Through

Strategy and tactics are both essential.

Abstract holds the values and direction. Concrete holds the execution and follow-through. Neither can function without the other. Name who holds what and stop competing.

Finding Synergy
When Different Parenting Styles Complete Each Other

Two parents with different parsers aren't undermining each other — they're covering different ground. The friction is actually the system working.

What Parent A Brings

Their parser's native strengths — whether that's long-range planning, emotional attunement, practical logistics, or big-picture values.

What Parent B Brings

The coordinates Parent A doesn't cover. If A is future-focused, B holds the history. If A tracks details, B holds meaning.

The Combined Effect

Kids get full coverage.

One parent isn't better. The children get what they need from each — different things from different parents. Stop trying to parent the same way and start parenting together.

Family Pattern Two

Parent ×
Child

Parent × Child · Domain Image
Face-Off #1
Other-Parent × Self-Child
The Setup

Parent tracks everyone — other kids, other families, what's appropriate. Child lives inside their own experience with no awareness of how their behavior affects others.

The Collision

"Why don't you think about other people?" The child isn't being selfish — they literally don't naturally track outward. The parent's constant corrections feel like rejection.

The Way Through

Teach the skill, don't demand the instinct.

Self-oriented kids can learn to track others — but it has to be taught explicitly, not expected naturally. "I need you to notice..." not "Why don't you ever..."

Face-Off #2
Concrete-Parent × Abstract-Child
The Setup

Parent deals in reality — grades, chores, schedules. Child lives in ideas — imagination, possibility, meaning. The parent sees avoidance. The child sees a prison of pointless tasks.

The Collision

"Just do what you're told" vs. "Why does it even matter?" Neither is being difficult. They're processing the same task through incompatible lenses.

The Way Through

Give the abstract child the why.

Abstract kids need to understand the meaning before they engage with the task. Take 30 seconds to explain the reason. It's not coddling — it's speaking their language.

Face-Off #3
Future-Parent × Present-Child
The Setup

Parent is always preparing — "You need to develop good habits for later." Child is fully here — "But I want to play now." Parent sees laziness. Child feels constant pressure.

The Collision

"You'll thank me later" vs. "There's always later." The future-oriented parent can't stop preparing. The present-oriented child can't see why now should be sacrificed for then.

The Way Through

Let them be present. Build the future anyway.

You can't make a present-child feel the future. Stop arguing about why it matters. Set the structure, let them live in it, and save the future-talk for abstract kids who care.

Finding Synergy
When Parent-Child Differences Create Growth

A parent with a different parser than their child isn't a problem — it's an opportunity. The child gets exposed to coordinates they wouldn't naturally develop on their own.

What the Parent Brings

Their parser's native strengths — modeled daily. The child absorbs what they see, even when it doesn't match their own lens.

What the Child Brings

A lens the parent doesn't have. The abstract child helps the concrete parent see meaning. The present child helps the future parent actually enjoy today.

The Combined Effect

The household develops range.

Different parsers in a family aren't a mistake. They're how families develop the full coordinate system — every lens represented, every strength modeled.

Family Pattern Three

Across
Generations

Generations · Domain Image
Face-Off #1
Past-Grandparent × Future-Parent
The Setup

Grandparent holds the traditions, the stories, the way things were. Parent is trying to build something new, prepare for what's coming. Both feel unseen by the other.

The Collision

"Why don't you do it the way we always have?" vs. "Things are different now." The grandparent feels erased. The parent feels controlled by the past.

The Way Through

Honor the roots while growing new branches.

Future doesn't have to mean forgetting. Past doesn't have to mean repeating. Build rituals that reference the old while serving the new.

Face-Off #2
The Skipped Generation
The Setup

Sometimes a grandparent and grandchild share a parser that the parent in the middle doesn't have. They "get" each other in ways that feel exclusive.

The Collision

The parent feels bypassed, left out, or undermined. The grandparent feels protective of the connection. The child is just being themselves with someone who matches their lens.

The Way Through

Parser alignment isn't betrayal.

A child bonding with a grandparent over shared coordinates isn't rejecting the parent. It's just easier to talk to someone who naturally matches your lens. Let it happen.

Finding Synergy
When Generations Create a Full Timeline

Three generations in one family often have different time orientations — past, present, future — giving the family unit a complete timeline.

What Older Generations Bring

Memory, continuity, wisdom earned from experience. The long view that says "this too shall pass" because they've seen it before.

What Younger Generations Bring

Vision, adaptation, the energy of becoming. The perspective that says "what if we tried it differently?" because they're not locked in yet.

The Combined Effect

A family that knows where it's been, where it is, and where it's going.

When all three time orientations are honored, the family has complete coverage. History without rigidity. Vision without rootlessness. Presence without shortsightedness.
