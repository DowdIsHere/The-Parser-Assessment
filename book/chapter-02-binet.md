# Chapter 2: The Personal Crisis That Transformed Alfred Binet

Alfred Binet did not arrive at psychology through curiosity. He arrived through failure.

## Collapse and Redirection

In 1879, at twenty-two, Binet attempted to follow his father and grandfathers into medicine. All three had been respected physicians. The path seemed settled. It wasn't. Binet suffered what contemporaries described as a severe nervous breakdown — an emotional and physical collapse that ended his medical training (Nicolas & Sanitioso, 2012). He later described that period as one of discouragement and disorientation, adrift in Paris without a clear direction.

A year before medicine, he had completed a law degree to satisfy family expectations. He practiced for several years and found it intolerable. He left that too (Wolf, 1973).

With both paths behind him, Binet withdrew to the reading rooms of the Bibliothèque Nationale. No professor. No program. No credential at the end. He read everything he could find in the emerging European literature on experimental psychology and taught himself the field from the ground up.

## A Father's Observation Becomes a Scientific Mission

The turn that mattered did not happen in a library or a laboratory.

It happened at home.

In 1887, Binet's younger daughter Alice was born. Her maternal grandfather, the eminent embryologist Édouard-Gérard Balbiani, made an observation that stayed with Binet. Alice appeared to have, in his words, *un papier brouillard sur l'intelligence* — a fog over her mind (M. Binet, 2011). The phrase was not a diagnosis. It did not suggest absence. It suggested something present but not fully accessible — intelligence organized or expressed differently than expected. Binet did not dismiss it. He began watching both daughters systematically, documenting how they approached problems, how they perceived, how they reasoned. In *L'Étude expérimentale de l'intelligence* (1903), he published those observations under pseudonyms — Armande for Madeleine, Marguerite for Alice — to protect their privacy (Binet, 1903).

Madeleine was methodical. She moved through tasks carefully, favoring structure and sequential reasoning. Alice was introspective, emotionally responsive, intuitive. Same household, same parents, same education. Fundamentally different cognitive orientations.

This was not a curiosity. For Binet, it was evidence. Intelligence was not singular. It varied in structure. It varied in expression. Any tool that reduced it to a single measure was not measuring it — it was misrepresenting it.

## Family Strain and Growing Urgency

After 1900, Binet's investment in these questions became impossible to separate from his personal life. His wife Laure experienced severe depression. The family became increasingly isolated. Both daughters were educated at home. Binet watched children — including his own — encounter a school system that sorted early and revised its judgments rarely.

His frustration is audible in *Modern Ideas About Children* (1909):

> "Never! What a strong word!… We must protest and react against this brutal pessimism… I have often observed, to my regret, that a widespread prejudice exists with regard to the educability of intelligence. These teachers lose interest in students with low intelligence… I have heard such rash statements too often."

This was not a theoretical argument. It was a father who had watched a system look at his daughter and see a fog, and had spent twenty years proving the fog was in the instrument, not the child.

## Professional Setback and Empathy for Failure

Binet's sensitivity to being wrong in public was also earned.

Between 1883 and 1890, he worked under Jean-Martin Charcot at the Salpêtrière Hospital, studying hypnosis and hysteria. Their research included phenomena associated with what was then called animal magnetism. Belgian psychologist Joseph Delboeuf later demonstrated that many of the observed effects were better explained by suggestion than by any underlying physical mechanism. Binet had been wrong. Visibly, professionally wrong.

He said so. He acknowledged the error publicly and resigned his position (Nicolas & Sanitioso, 2012).

That experience shaped everything that followed. It deepened his skepticism toward authoritative claims. It gave him a particular sensitivity to how easily a person — or a child — could be misread and then dismissed on the basis of that misreading.

## Building a Test for Support, Not Judgment

In 1904, the French Ministry of Education asked Binet to serve on a commission studying children with learning difficulties. The dominant view at the time, represented by psychiatrist Désiré-Magloire Bourneville among others, favored institutional placement — removing struggling children from the school system.

Binet argued for the opposite. He proposed *classes de perfectionnement* — specialized educational environments within schools, designed to support development rather than redirect children out of it.

Working with physician Théodore Simon, he developed what became the Binet-Simon scale: a set of tasks designed to identify where a child currently was in order to guide what came next. The purpose was not classification. It was not ranking. It was not the measurement of fixed capacity.

Binet was direct about this:

> "With practice, training, and above all, method, we manage to increase our attention, our memory, our judgment, and literally to become more intelligent than we were before" (Binet, 1909).

The scale was a starting point. A map of current function, used to plan the next step.

## The Transformation of His Work

Binet died in 1911, before his scale had traveled far. Within a few years, Lewis Terman at Stanford adapted it into a form designed to measure what he called native intelligence — the capacity a person was born with, relatively stable over time (Terman, 1916). The Binet-Simon scale became the Stanford-Binet. The purpose quietly inverted: from a tool for identifying where to begin, to a tool for determining what a person was.

What Binet built to open doors became, in other hands, a mechanism for closing them.

## Closing Note

Binet's framework did not emerge from detached observation. It was built by a man who had failed publicly, watched his daughter be misread, and spent his career arguing that the fog was never in the child. What he created was not designed to define limits. It was designed to find where to start — and to insist that starting was always possible.
