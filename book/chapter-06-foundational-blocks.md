# Chapter 6: The "Foundational" Blocks — And Why That Word Misleads

Neuroscience has documented a consistent developmental pattern. Primary sensory cortices reach peak synaptic density early and undergo substantial pruning in childhood, while prefrontal association areas continue myelinating and reorganizing into early adulthood (Huttenlocher & Dabholkar, 1997; Giedd et al., 1999; Casey et al., 2005). This timeline appears to support a strict hierarchical model: unimodal sensory processing first, followed by motor coordination, language, and finally abstract reasoning.

The model is intuitive. It is also incomplete.

Functional evidence from infant cognition shows measurable signatures of executive attention, working memory, pattern learning, and social inference within the first year of life (Spelke & Kinzler, 2007; Baillargeon, 2004). At the same time, capacities traditionally labeled foundational continue refining well into adolescence.

Three processes — perceptual discrimination, sequential processing, and spatial relationships — have long been labeled foundational because they become measurable early and are tightly coupled to raw input. The label implies they develop first and serve as prerequisites for all other cognition.

They do not.

These three processes are the most direct functional expressions of three independent organizing gradients that orient every signal before any block-level processing begins. The gradients are the actual coordinate infrastructure.

- The **anterior–posterior axis (Temporal gradient)** biases signals toward prospective versus retrospective processing windows. Anterior hippocampal regions show longer temporal integration; posterior regions anchor more strongly to immediate or recent input. Hippocampal time cells encode scalar time along this axis (Eichenbaum, 2014).
- The **ventral–dorsal axis (Spatial gradient)** runs from ventral stream processing of object features ("what") to dorsal stream processing of relational and spatial information ("where/how") (Mishkin et al., 1983; Goodale & Milner, 1992).
- The **medial–lateral axis (Reference gradient)** orients signals toward internal/self-referenced (medial, default-mode network heavy) versus external/other-referenced frames.

These gradients align with the principal gradient of macroscale cortical organization, which runs from unimodal sensory-motor regions to transmodal association regions (Margulies et al., 2016). They operate as independent, high-speed streams that converge primarily at hippocampal and transmodal hubs rather than intercepting en route.

The three so-called foundational blocks are early-visible precisely because they sit closest to this raw oriented input. They are not the structural foundation. The gradients that orient every signal before block engagement are the foundation.

Development is not the sequential construction of new capacities from the ground up. It is the progressive refinement of how gradient-oriented signals are expressed through the blocks — increases in accessibility, integration depth, and efficiency governed by theta–gamma phase-amplitude coupling precision at convergence points.

Binet observed different natural relationships to these same gradients in his daughters. Madeleine's methodical sequencing reflected strong coupling along the anterior–posterior axis. Alice's pattern-driven, configurational insight reflected dominant coordination along the ventral–dorsal axis. He described these as differences in cognitive method. He was observing gradient variation without the anatomical map that would name it.

The three gradient-proximate blocks are real and centrally important. Their early observability is real. What they are not is the bottom of the architecture.

What these gradients are, how they direct signals, and what observable outputs emerge when blocks operate within their directional flows is the subject of the next chapter.
