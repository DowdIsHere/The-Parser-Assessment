# Chapter 4: Intelligence by Definition

## Definitional Drift

For over a century, psychology has attempted to define intelligence.

The American Psychological Association noted that "two dozen prominent theorists gave two dozen somewhat different definitions" (Neisser et al., 1996, p. 77).

This is not a failure.

It is what happens when a complex phenomenon is examined from multiple vantage points, by researchers whose tools shaped what they could see.

Howard Gardner emphasized culturally valued forms of ability. Robert Sternberg focused on adaptation and problem-solving in real environments. Daniel Goleman brought emotional awareness into the conversation. Charles Spearman identified a general cognitive factor underlying performance across tasks. Carroll and the later CHC framework mapped hierarchies of broad and narrow abilities.

Each model is valid within its scope.

Each reveals part of the terrain.

Each is also bounded by what it chose to measure — and by what its instruments could reach.

## The Dictionary Baseline

Standard definitions offer a useful point of departure.

Merriam-Webster defines intelligence as the ability to learn, understand, and adapt; the skilled use of reason; and the ability to apply knowledge in new or difficult situations.

Across these definitions, a consistent pattern emerges.

Intelligence is not what you know.

It is how you process, apply, and adapt knowledge when conditions change.

Cognition is typically defined as "cognitive mental processes" — a definition that is circular. It names what we are trying to understand without clarifying what those processes actually are.

That clarification is what this chapter attempts.

## From Output to Process

Most assessment measures outcomes.

Correct answers. Reaction times. Recalled items. Scores.

These are useful. They are real signals of something.

But they are outputs.

They do not reveal how those outputs were produced.

Two individuals can arrive at the same answer through entirely different internal processes. One through sequential elimination. One through pattern recognition. One through spatial visualization. The score is identical. The cognition is not.

Understanding intelligence requires moving beneath the output — from what was produced to how it was produced.

This is not a methodological preference. It is a structural requirement. If the goal is to understand cognition rather than simply rank it, the processes themselves must be the object of study.

## Smart vs. Intelligent

Language gives us a distinction that formal models often collapse.

Smart describes performance within known structures. The rules are clear, the path is established, and the person navigating it does so efficiently and accurately.

Intelligent describes performance when structure is unclear, absent, or must be created. The rules are not given. The path does not exist yet. The person must produce it.

Smartness operates within established systems. Intelligence operates when those systems must be navigated, adapted, or built from the available materials.

Most standardized testing measures the former. It assesses how well someone performs within a known structure. Less often does it capture what a person does when the structure is missing.

This is not a criticism of standardized testing. It is a description of what it was designed to do — and what falls outside its reach.

## The Measurement Gap

Alfred Binet did not design his scale to define limits.

He designed it to understand how individual children learned, so instruction could be adjusted to meet them. As discussed in Chapter 3, the transformation from that diagnostic tool into a ranking instrument was not Binet's design. It was what happened to his design when it was adapted for mass administration under wartime constraints.

That adaptation emphasized the cognitive functions that could be measured quickly, scored objectively, and applied at scale.

Pattern completion. Symbolic reasoning. Short-term recall. Processing speed.

These are genuine cognitive functions. Measuring them is not wrong.

But the functions that could not survive the adaptation — generative thinking, self-reflection, social interpretation — did not become less real. They became less visible. And over time, what was visible became what was measured. What was measured became what intelligence meant.

The gap between those two things — what intelligence is and what intelligence tests measure — is the subject this book addresses.

## Toward a Process-Level View

If intelligence is not a single ability, and if output measures alone cannot reveal how cognition operates, then a different level of analysis is required.

That level is process.

Across domains, certain cognitive operations recur. They appear in language, in mathematics, in social interaction, in creative work, in clinical judgment, in athletic performance. They combine differently depending on context. They express differently depending on individual architecture. But they recur.

Recognizing patterns. Organizing sequences. Distinguishing signals from noise. Navigating spatial relationships. Manipulating symbols. Generating ideas that do not yet exist. Evaluating one's own thinking. Interpreting the signals other people produce.

These are not domain-specific abilities. They are the operations that make domain-specific ability possible.

The domain changes. The underlying processes recur.

## The CBI Framework

The Cognition Blocks of Intelligence framework organizes these recurring processes into eight functional categories.

**Pattern Recognition** — identifying meaningful configurations across experience.

**Sequential Processing** — organizing information and action in temporal order.

**Spatial Relationships** — computing position, distance, and spatial structure.

**Perceptual Discrimination** — detecting and distinguishing features in sensory input.

**Symbolic Manipulation** — operating on representations of meaning: language, mathematics, code.

**Generative Creation** — producing outputs that do not yet exist.

**Self-Reference Processing** — monitoring and interpreting one's own cognitive states.

**Social Signal Processing** — detecting and interpreting the behavioral and emotional signals of others.

These eight are not a new theory of intelligence positioned against existing ones.

They are a process-level description of what existing theories have always been pointing toward.

Gardner's musical intelligence involves pattern recognition, sequential processing, and perceptual discrimination applied in the auditory domain. His spatial intelligence involves spatial relationships, perceptual discrimination, and pattern recognition applied visually. Goleman's emotional intelligence involves social signal processing, self-reference processing, and pattern recognition applied interpersonally.

The domain changes. The processes recur.

CBI does not replace these frameworks. It operates one level beneath them — describing the processes that combine to produce what those frameworks describe as abilities.

## From Measurement to Understanding

If intelligence is expressed through combinations of underlying processes, then measuring those processes directly offers something current assessments do not.

It identifies not just where performance falls, but which processes are contributing to it and which are not.

It distinguishes between a student who cannot read because phonological processing is underdeveloped and one who cannot read because sequential processing is the bottleneck and pattern recognition is intact and waiting to be used differently.

Those are not the same situation. They do not have the same solution.

Logan's educators knew he could not read. The instruments available to them could not tell them why — because the processes that explained his difficulty were not part of what those instruments measured.

That is the gap. And it is not small.

## Understanding Intelligence as Diverse Orientation

1. **Holistic Intelligence:** Intelligence encompasses a range of perceptual focuses, including how individuals prioritize time (past or future), space, and self-awareness.

2. **Cognitive Styles:** People exhibit different thinking styles — some are concrete, engaging with specifics, while others are abstract, seeing broader patterns and relationships.

3. **Self vs. Other Orientation:** Intelligence also manifests in how individuals relate to themselves and others, balancing self-centeredness with selflessness.

4. **Dynamic Interplay:** These varied orientations collectively shape how individuals perceive and interact with the world, highlighting that intelligence is a multifaceted and dynamic construct.

This is what Binet saw in 1905. This is what got lost in 1917. This is what we can finally measure properly.

The eight cognitive processes aren't hidden. They've been operating all along, in every human mind, in every culture, across every domain. We simply stopped measuring most of them.

It's time to measure all of them again.

## Closing

Intelligence has been defined in many ways.

Each definition is real. Each captures something the others do not emphasize. The field is richer for the disagreement.

But definitions describe what intelligence is.

They do not explain how it operates.

To understand intelligence more completely, we must look beneath the definitions — to the processes that produce the outcomes those definitions describe.

Not to replace what has been built.

To see what has always been there.
