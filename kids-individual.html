Is Your Kid

Your child's parser isn't a personality type or a learning style. It's the angle from which they take in reality — what they notice first, before thinking even begins.

01
The Three Gradients

Three questions.
Three positions.

Every parser is defined by where a child sits on three independent gradients. Each gradient has two poles and a balanced middle. 3 × 3 × 3 = 27 unique combinations.

Gradient 01

Spatial

Does this child trust what they can see — or what they perceive and imagine?

Concrete What they can see. What's right in front of them. Real, touchable, provable.
Abstract What they perceive or imagine. Patterns, possibilities, the bigger picture.
Balanced Moves between both depending on what the moment needs.
Gradient 02

Temporal

Does this child orient toward what happened before — or what's going to happen?

Past What happened before. Experience, memory, tradition, proof.
Future What's going to happen. Anticipation, planning, possibility.
Present What's happening right now. The moment itself.
Gradient 03

Reference

Does this child process from inside themselves — or from reading others?

Self Themselves. Their own experience, their own logic, their own anchor.
Other Others. The room, the group, the people around them.
Balanced Draws from both sources, neither dominant.

What You Get

A two-part report.
One for them. One for you.

The Kid Parser Profile is built in two halves — one written directly to your child, one written directly to you.

Part One · For Your Child
Written to them — in language they can hear.
I

Your Mind

A plain-language explanation of how their specific lens works — what they notice first, why certain things come easily, and why that's not a mistake.

II

Your Strengths

The specific abilities their parser gives them — named clearly, with examples they'll recognize from their own life.

III

What Helps You

The environments, habits, and conditions where their mind does its best work.

IV

What's Hard

The genuine friction their wiring creates — named honestly, without shame, with strategies that actually fit how they think.

V

The One Thing

One sentence that captures the essential truth of how their mind works.

Part Two · For You, the Parent
Written to you — with everything you need.
I

What You're Looking At

A parent-level explanation of your child's parser — what it means in practice, day to day.

II

The Gift

What this parser gives your child that others don't have — the real advantage, named so you can protect it.

III

The Friction

What looks like misbehavior but isn't — and what actually is. The distinction changes how you respond.

IV

How to Discipline

What works, what doesn't, the phrase that stops them, and the phrase that makes it worse.

V

How to Teach

The learning conditions and approaches that actually work for this specific mind.

VI

Your Parser vs. Theirs

How your own parser interacts with theirs — where you'll connect and where you'll accidentally create friction.

VII

When They Shut Down

What happens when your child blocks — the specific pattern, the trigger, and the way back through.

VIII

The One Thing

The single most important thing to keep in mind about your child's mind.

04
When Kid Parsers Collide

The geometry of
kid friction.

Most friction between kids and parents, siblings, or classmates isn't about behavior. It's about two people processing the same moment from different positions on the same three gradients.

Spatial Mismatch

Concrete vs. Abstract

Parent: "You're not getting the point!"
Child: "Show me what you mean."

One speaks in principles. One needs examples. Neither is wrong.

Temporal Mismatch

Past vs. Future

Parent: "You never appreciate what you have!"
Child: "I'm excited about what's next."

One honors what was. One reaches for what's coming. Neither is ungrateful.

Reference Mismatch

Self vs. Other

Parent: "Why are you so selfish?"
Child: "I'm just figuring out what I think."

One processes outward. One processes inward. Neither is uncaring.

Domain One

Parent–Child Collisions

Spatial Mismatch

Concrete Parent · Abstract Child

Parent

"Stop overcomplicating everything!"

Child

"I'm seeing patterns you're missing."

The fix: Ask the child to walk you down to an example. "Show me what that would look like." Bridge the altitude without dismissing either floor.

Spatial Mismatch

Abstract Parent · Concrete Child

Parent

"You're not getting the point!"

Child

"Just show me what to do."

The fix: Translate the concept into an example. Show, don't explain. The understanding comes after the experience, not before.

Temporal Mismatch

Past Parent · Future Child

Parent

"You never appreciate what you have!"

Child

"I'm excited about what's next!"

The fix: Connect future goals to past lessons. "You're right that there's more ahead — and here's what you've already learned that will help."

Temporal Mismatch

Future Parent · Past Child

Parent

"Let it go — move forward!"

Child

"Why do you want me to forget?"

The fix: Don't rush them forward — walk them there. "That mattered. And here's where we go from here."

Reference Mismatch

Other Parent · Self Child

Parent

"Why are you so selfish?"

Child

"I'm figuring out what I think."

The fix: Self-reference isn't selfishness. Invite them in rather than demanding they arrive pre-connected. "Tell me what you think" works better than "Think about others."

Reference Mismatch

Self Parent · Other Child

Parent

"Stop worrying about everyone else."

Child

"I can't not notice."

The fix: Attunement isn't weakness. Help them learn boundaries without telling them their wiring is wrong. "You noticed that — what do you want to do about it?"

Domain Two

Sibling Collisions

Spatial Mismatch

Concrete Sibling · Abstract Sibling

Concrete

"You always make stuff up!"

Abstract

"You never want to try anything new!"

The fix: Give them different roles. Let the concrete kid be the quality checker, the abstract kid be the idea starter. Both positions are honored.

Temporal Mismatch

Past Sibling · Future Sibling

Past

"We always do it this way!"

Future

"That's boring — let's change it!"

The fix: Keep the core of the tradition but let the future sibling remix the edges. Honor what was while reaching for what could be.

Reference Mismatch

Self Sibling · Other Sibling

Self

"Leave me alone, I'm thinking!"

Other

"Talk to me! I can tell something's wrong!"

The fix: Give them a signal system. "I need space" and "I'm here when you're ready." One reaches out, the other reaches in — both are connecting.

Domain Three

Classroom Collisions

Spatial Mismatch

Abstract Teacher · Concrete Student

Teacher

"Think about the concept."

Student

"I need to see an example."

The fix: This child isn't slow — they're grounded. They'll understand the concept after they've seen it work three times, not before.

Temporal Mismatch

Present Teacher · Past Student

Teacher

"Focus on what we're doing now."

Student

"But you have to understand why."

The fix: Give this student the backstory first. They need to know how this connects to what came before — then the present makes sense.

Reference Mismatch

Group Work · Self Student

Teacher

"Collaborate with your group."

Student

"I think better alone."

The fix: Let them think first, then share. Self-processing students contribute more when they've had solo time before the group converges.
