History's Clearest Collisions

When the pattern is undeniable.

Some conflicts are so clean on one axis that they become teaching cases. Different architectures, same moment, inevitable friction.

Jobs vs. Sculley · Apple 1985
Temporal Collision

Steve Jobs vs. John Sculley

Apple, 1985

Jobs was future-anchored — building the Mac for a market that didn't exist yet, willing to cannibalize the Apple II. Sculley was present/past-anchored — protecting current revenue, reading market data, managing what was working.

The Collision

"We need to invest in where this is going" vs. "We need to protect what's paying the bills right now."

Jobs got fired. Then the future arrived.

Musk vs. Advertisers · Twitter 2022
Reference Collision

Elon Musk vs. Twitter Advertisers

Twitter/X, 2022–23

Musk is self-referential — acts on internal logic regardless of stakeholder reaction. Advertisers are other-referential — brand safety, public perception, relationship maintenance.

The Collision

"I'm doing what I think is right" vs. "You're ignoring everyone who has to approve this."

Advertisers pulled spend. Musk called it extortion.

Holmes vs. Scientists · Theranos
Spatial Collision

Theranos Board vs. Lab Scientists

Theranos, 2014–2018

Holmes spoke in pure abstraction — "democratizing healthcare," "a world where no one has to say goodbye too soon." The scientists needed concrete — working assays, reproducible results, actual specifications.

The Collision

"You're not seeing the vision" vs. "You're not seeing the data."

The abstraction was never grounded. The concrete was never heard.

The Three Axes

Anatomy of a collision.

Every parser collision happens along one or more of three independent axes. Different combinations produce different friction patterns.

Axis One

Temporal Collisions

Past ↔ Present ↔ Future

One person is already in next quarter while another is still processing last week. Different time orientations create fundamental friction about what matters now.

Axis Two

Spatial Collisions

Concrete ↔ Balanced ↔ Abstract

One person speaks in principles while another needs the specific example. Different abstraction levels create friction about what counts as "clear."

Axis Three

Reference Collisions

Self ↔ Balanced ↔ Other

One person tracks every stakeholder while another just wants to focus on the work. Different reference frames create friction about what's "relevant."
