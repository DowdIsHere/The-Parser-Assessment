02
The Geometry

Why some friendships
feel like work.

The friction isn't about compatibility. It's about geometry. Two parsers processing the same friendship through completely different architectures.

Social Pattern One

The Initiation
Gap

Initiation Gap · Domain Image
Face-Off #1
Future × Present
The Setup

One friend is always planning — "We should do this sometime, let's organize that trip." The other lives in the now — they're great when you're together but never initiate.

The Collision

Future feels like they're doing all the work. Present feels pressured by constant planning. "Why don't you ever reach out?" vs. "Why can't we just be in the moment?"

The Way Through

Different initiation styles, not different commitment levels.

Future initiates by planning. Present initiates by showing up fully when it happens. Neither is investing less — they're investing differently. Name it and stop keeping score.

Face-Off #2
Other × Self
The Setup

One friend tracks everyone — remembers birthdays, notices when you're off, always checking in. The other is warm when together but doesn't track between hangouts.

The Collision

Other feels like the friendship is one-sided. Self has no idea anything is wrong — they thought everything was fine. "You never ask about my life" vs. "I didn't know you were going through that."

The Way Through

Other-oriented friends track automatically. Self-oriented friends track when prompted.

If you're Other, tell Self what you need: "Check in on me sometimes." If you're Self, build the prompt into your system: "I should text them this week."

Finding Synergy
When Different Initiation Styles Complete Each Other

One friend holds the thread between hangouts. One friend brings full presence when you're together. Both are real investment — just in different currencies.

What Future/Other Brings

Continuity. The plan that makes it happen. The check-in that keeps the connection alive between moments.

What Present/Self Brings

Full arrival. No half-presence. When they're there, they're actually there — undistracted, engaged, real.

The Combined Effect

A friendship with both maintenance and depth.

One keeps it alive. One makes it matter when it happens. Stop tracking who does what and start valuing what each brings.

Social Pattern Two

The Depth
Mismatch

Depth Mismatch · Domain Image
Face-Off #1
Abstract × Concrete
The Setup

One friend wants to talk about ideas, meaning, the big picture. The other wants to talk about what actually happened — the details, the story, the specific.

The Collision

Abstract feels bored by the play-by-play. Concrete feels dismissed by the jump to pattern. "Can we get to the point?" vs. "You're not even listening to what happened."

The Way Through

Both are real ways of connecting.

Concrete tells the story. Abstract draws the meaning. Take turns: let the details land, then zoom out. Neither is more sophisticated.

Face-Off #2
Past × Future
The Setup

One friend processes by talking through what happened. The other wants to skip to what's next. Different ways of making sense of things.

The Collision

"Why are we still talking about this?" vs. "Why don't you ever let me finish processing?" Future feels stuck. Past feels rushed.

The Way Through

Let Past complete. Then let Future plan.

Rushing Past creates resentment. Trapping Future creates restlessness. Take turns. "I need to finish this first" / "Okay, then let's talk about what's next."

Finding Synergy
When Different Depths Create Range

One friend holds the story. One friend sees the arc. Together the friendship has both memory and direction.

What Concrete/Past Brings

The record. The shared history. "Remember when..." The richness that comes from actually tracking what happened.

What Abstract/Future Brings

The trajectory. Where the friendship is going. What it could become. The vision that keeps it growing.

The Combined Effect

A friendship that knows where it's been and where it's going.

History without vision stagnates. Vision without history floats. Together you get both — a friendship with roots and direction.

Social Pattern Three

Group
Dynamics

Group Dynamics · Domain Image
Face-Off #1
Self × Other in Groups
The Setup

Self-oriented shows up as themselves regardless of the room. Other-oriented reads and adapts to the group energy. Both think the other is doing it wrong.

The Collision

"Why do you change who you are depending on who you're with?" vs. "Why don't you ever read the room?" Self seems rigid. Other seems fake. Neither is true.

The Way Through

Consistency isn't stubbornness. Adaptation isn't inauthenticity.

Self brings the same person to every room — that's stability. Other brings the version of themselves that serves the room — that's attunement. Both are real.

Face-Off #2
The Spotlight Question
The Setup

Some parsers naturally draw attention. Others fade into the background. In groups, this creates dynamics that neither may fully see.

The Collision

The spotlight person doesn't notice they're taking all the oxygen. The background person doesn't realize they're retreating. Neither is trying to dominate or disappear.

The Way Through

Make the invisible visible.

If you draw attention, actively redirect it: "What do you think?" If you fade, practice taking space: "I have something to add." Both can learn the other's move.

Finding Synergy
When Different Group Styles Create Balance

Some friends anchor a group. Others read it. Together you get both — someone to hold the center and someone to track the edges.

What Self/Center Brings

An anchor. A stable presence people can orient around. Someone who doesn't shift with every wind.

What Other/Edge Brings

Awareness. Who's being left out, what's not being said, where the tension is. The radar that keeps the group healthy.

The Combined Effect

A friend group with both stability and sensitivity.

The center holds. The edges get watched. No one dominates and no one disappears. That's geometric balance.
