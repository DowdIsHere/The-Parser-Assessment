# CBI Tennis Parser Behavior Playbook

**Version 2.1**

*Companion to the per-profile reference, "Parser Profiles — Tennis Players (Book Reference)." That document tells you what each of the 27 Parsers* is *as a player. This one is the engine: how to* read *a Parser from behavior, then predict the matchup.*

-----

## Core Rule

A Parser is **architecture** — the oriented slice of reality that becomes available to a player first, before conscious choice.

You read it **from sustained behavior**: what a player reaches for again and again, across opponents and across pressure. One point proves nothing. A pattern that recurs is the Parser showing itself.

What a Parser is *not*: age, stats, ranking, surface, handedness, or score. Those are **conditions**. They don't identify the Parser — they pressure it.

Hold three things at once:

- **Parser identity** tells you what becomes available first.
- **Conditions** tell you how costly that orientation becomes.
- **The opposing Parser** tells you where the misread will happen.

**The one discipline that keeps a read honest:** separate *architecture* from *condition-forced behavior*. A player dragged off their game by a bad matchup, a hostile crowd, or a 0–5 scoreline will briefly behave like a different Parser. Read the Parser from what **recurs** when the player is free to play their own way — not from a single stretch where conditions are dictating.

## 1. Parser → Tennis Behavior Mapping

Each gradient expresses as one observable behavior set on court.

### Reference — *Who is the match organized around?*

**Self = Force Game**
Organizes from their own game. Imposes rhythm, pressure, patterns, shot selection, pace.
*Question:* "What can I make happen?"

**Other = Play Other's Game**
Organizes around the opponent. Reads discomfort, habits, openings, reactions, vulnerabilities.
*Question:* "What are you showing me?"

**Balanced = The Flow**
Organizes through the exchange itself — neither purely imposing nor purely reacting. Moves with the match as it develops.
*Question:* "What is the point asking for now?"
*Distinction from the Options Player:* The Flow operates in the **live exchange** — it reads what the rally is becoming as it unfolds. The Options Player operates in **possibility space** — it weighs which lines are playable. The Flow is present-tense engagement; the Options Player steps back to assess. They can look similar from the outside but resolve differently under pressure: The Flow stays inside the moment; the Options Player steps back to choose.

-----

### Temporal — *When does the player live?*

**Future = Proactive**
Acts before the thing fully arrives. Moves toward what is forming — takes time away, first-strike, plays to where the ball will be.
*Question:* "Where is this going?"

**Past = Reactive**
Works from what has already happened. Uses record, pattern, repetition, proof, prior response. Counterpunches; waits for the known shape.
*Question:* "What has already shown itself?"

**Present = Vibing**
Inside the live moment. Feels timing, rhythm, current cues, immediate flow. Carries nothing forward and projects nothing ahead.
*Question:* "What is happening right now?"

-----

### Spatial — *How does the player handle the board?*

**Abstract = The Possibility Player**
Plays toward what could open before it is fully visible. Manufactures shots that aren't there yet; opens the court ahead of the evidence. High ceiling, high variance.
*Question:* "What can this become?"
*Risk:* May try to open the court before it has earned the opening.

**Balanced = The Options Player**
Holds both the confirmed and the possible. Keeps multiple lines available and shifts between them without becoming chaotic.
*Question:* "Which line is playable from here?"
*Strength:* Usually has exits. Can change direction without falling apart.

**Concrete = The Sequence Player**
Works from confirmed circumstances — established patterns, known positions, proven weaknesses, reliable sequences. Builds the point brick by brick toward a finish they've run before. Low variance.
*Question:* "Given what's confirmed, what sequence follows?"
*Risk:* Needs the board to stay stable long enough for the sequence to mature.

> **v2.1 note.** Earlier drafts named the Spatial poles "Ambitious / Calculated / Chess Player." Those are retired. "Chess Player" wrongly implied the *concrete* pole was the strategist, when point-construction at altitude is the *abstract* end; and "Calculated" had imported conditions (surface, fatigue, score) into what must be a pure abstraction stance. Conditions now live only in the read steps below, never in the Parser definition.

## 2. Reading the Parser from Behavior

This is the evaluation engine. Watch the player for a set or two and answer each gradient independently. Read what **recurs**, not what a single point shows.

### Reference: Force Game vs Play Other's Game vs The Flow

| You see… | Read |
|---|---|
| Shot selection stays the same across different opponents; plays the big point with their own play; dictates pace and goes to their weapon regardless of matchup; problem-solves inward | **Force Game (Self)** — "I'll beat you with my game" |
| Shot selection visibly changes by opponent; hunts a specific weakness; uses junk, variety, disguise; reads body language; feeds off (or is rattled by) the crowd | **Play Other's Game (Other)** — "I'll beat you with your weakness" |
| Neither a fixed signature pattern nor a fixed opponent-target; lets the rally set the terms, then redirects; mirrors tempo and finds the seam mid-exchange | **The Flow (Balanced)** — "I'll beat you with the rally" |

**Quick test:** Track shot selection across two different opponents (or two clearly different phases). *Constant* = Self. *Tracks the opponent* = Other. *Tracks the developing shape of the rally* = Balanced.

-----

### Temporal: Proactive vs Reactive vs Vibing

| You see… | Read |
|---|---|
| Takes the ball early, robs time, first-strikes, ends points moving forward, plays "ahead of" the rally | **Proactive (Future)** |
| Counterpunches, absorbs pace, plays off what's already shown, waits for the established pattern, extends to draw the known error | **Reactive (Past)** |
| Rhythm- and feel-driven, streaky, swings with momentum, lives point-to-point, neither carrying the last point nor projecting the next | **Vibing (Present)** |

**Quick test:** Watch how they handle a momentum shift. *Re-plans toward where it's heading* = Future. *Leans on what's worked before* = Past. *Just plays the next ball on feel* = Present.

-----

### Spatial: Possibility Player vs Options Player vs Sequence Player

| You see… | Read |
|---|---|
| Runs proven patterns to completion; predictable in a disciplined way; builds brick by brick; low variance; needs stable conditions | **Sequence Player (Concrete)** |
| Shows several looks; has a Plan B mid-point; shifts cleanly without unraveling; moderate variance | **Options Player (Balanced)** |
| Goes for shots that aren't there yet; opens the court before it's earned; spectacular and erratic in the same set; high variance | **Possibility Player (Abstract)** |

**Quick test:** On a neutral ball, does the player run a *known sequence* (Concrete), *pick among several lines* (Balanced), or *try to manufacture something not yet available* (Abstract)?

-----

### Synthesis

Stack the three reads into one Parser:

> Spatial • Temporal • Reference
> e.g., **Possibility Player • Proactive • Force Game** = Abstract • Future • Self = **Visionary** (The Point Architect)
> e.g., **Sequence Player • Reactive • Play Other's Game** = Concrete • Past • Other = **Legacy** (The Team Standard-Bearer)

Cross-reference the resulting code to the 27 profiles in the companion reference for the full behavioral picture, signature game, and breakdown points.

## 3. How to Read a Match

### Step 1: Read each player's Parser from behavior

Use Section 2. Identify each gradient from what recurs across opponents and pressure. Don't lock a read from a single condition-forced stretch — confirm it repeats when the player is free to play their own way.

### Step 2: Add the conditions

Conditions don't identify the Parser — they pressure it. Ask:

- Does this surface reward the Parser's natural behavior?
- Does the opponent's hand disrupt it?
- Does the score make the Parser's normal move expensive?
- Does experience help stabilize the behavior?
- Does fatigue make the Parser overuse its strength?

### Step 3: Predict the misread

The misread is not random — it comes from Parser-vs-Parser orientation. Ask:

- What will this player think the opponent is doing?
- What part of the opponent's behavior becomes visible first?
- What causal piece might they miss?
- Are they mistaking a response for a pattern?
- Are they waiting for a behavior they were previously causing?

### Step 4: Watch the causal loop

The scoreboard is noise unless it connects to the loop. The real questions:

- Who is causing the other player to shift?
- Does the other player know what caused the shift?
- Has the player adapted, or are they solving the previous version of the match?

## 4. Temporal Collision Rules

### Future vs Past
Future moves with trajectory. Past turns behavior into record.
- **Future wins when:** the trajectory keeps changing before Past can use the record.
- **Past wins when:** Future's patterns become repeatable enough to be archived.
*Clean line:* Past catches Future only when Future leaves enough history.

### Future vs Present
Future sees where the match is going. Present feels what is happening now.
- **Future wins when:** the match moves before Present can act on the cue.
- **Present wins when:** Future leaks enough live evidence to be caught.
*Clean line:* Present cannot see the future, but it can catch the traces forming now.

### Past vs Present
Past uses what's confirmed. Present moves with the live moment.
- **Past wins when:** the present keeps repeating known patterns.
- **Present wins when:** the live situation contradicts the record.
*Clean line:* Past owns repetition. Present owns the moment repetition breaks.

### Same-Temporal Matches
When both players share temporal orientation, the temporal dimension becomes invisible. The match resolves on Spatial and Reference — pure architecture, because neither side holds a temporal edge.

-----

## 5. Spatial Collision Rules

### Possibility Player vs Sequence Player (Abstract vs Concrete)
Possibility changes the trajectory. Sequence builds from confirmed steps.
- **Possibility wins when:** the board changes before the sequence matures.
- **Sequence wins when:** possibility becomes repeatable enough to confirm.
*Clean line:* Sequence can solve the board. Possibility keeps changing what the board is becoming.

### Possibility Player vs Options Player (Abstract vs Balanced)
Possibility sees what could become possible. Options sees which possibilities are actually playable.
- **Possibility wins when:** the player expands the match beyond current constraints.
- **Options wins when:** the player knows which possibilities survive contact.
*Clean line:* Possibility opens futures. Options knows which futures survive contact.

### Options Player vs Sequence Player (Balanced vs Concrete)
Options keeps lines open. Sequence drives one proven line to its end.
- **Options wins when:** the player changes the equation before the confirmed sequence completes.
- **Sequence wins when:** the player locks the match into proven cause-and-effect.
*Clean line:* Options has exits. Sequence has sequences.

### Same-Spatial Matches
When both players share spatial orientation, the spatial dimension becomes invisible. Two Possibility Players play a match of escalating possibility. Two Sequence Players play a match of confirmed sequences. Two Options Players play a match of mutual exits. The decisive friction shifts to Temporal and Reference.

-----

## 6. Reference Collision Rules

### Force Game vs Play Other's Game (Self vs Other)
Force Game imposes. Play Other's Game reads.
- **Force Game wins when:** the imposed pressure creates more than the opponent can organize.
- **Play Other's Game wins when:** the imposed pressure becomes readable.
*Clean line:* Self creates the problem. Other tries to make the problem readable.

### Force Game vs The Flow (Self vs Balanced)
Force Game shapes the match. The Flow moves with it.
- **Force Game wins when:** the player's pressure becomes the match structure.
- **The Flow wins when:** the match refuses to stay inside the forced structure.
*Clean line:* Self tries to own the match. Flow waits for the match to stop belonging to Self.

### Play Other's Game vs The Flow (Other vs Balanced)
Play Other's Game needs an opponent to read. The Flow doesn't always give a stable target.
- **Play Other's Game wins when:** the opponent becomes consistent enough to map.
- **The Flow wins when:** the match keeps changing without offering a fixed person-pattern to exploit.
*Clean line:* Other reads the player. Flow reads the exchange.

### Same-Reference Matches
When both players share reference orientation, the reference dimension becomes invisible. Two Force Game players play a match of mutual imposition — whoever's pressure structures the match wins. Two Play Other's Game players play a match of mutual reading — whoever stays illegible longer wins. Two Flow players play a match where the rally itself becomes the player. The decisive friction shifts to Temporal and Spatial.

## 8. Core Warnings

**Read the Parser from behavior — never from a condition.** Conditions describe the situation, not the architecture.

**Do not say:**
- "He's young, so he's Future."
- "She plays clay, so she's Concrete."
- "He's ranked higher, so he's Options."
- "She's left-handed, so she's a Sequence Player."

Those are conditions wearing a Parser's clothes.

**Do say:**
- "Across three opponents his shot selection didn't change — that's Force Game."
- "She keeps taking the ball early to rob time — that's Proactive."
- "He runs the same two patterns to completion and needs the court stable — Sequence Player."

**And keep the read honest:**
- Don't lock a Parser from a single condition-forced stretch — a player buried at 0–5 or eaten alive by a bad matchup will briefly mimic another Parser.
- Confirm the read by watching what **recurs** when the player is free to play their own way.
- Don't mistake a one-off response for a standing pattern, and don't keep reading a player you've already caused to change.

-----

## 9. Cleanest Summary

- Parser identity is architecture — and you read it from behavior that recurs.
- Parser behavior is architecture under pressure.
- Conditions shape expression; they don't define the Parser.
- The opponent's Parser predicts the misread.
- The match resolves when one player's Parser is operating in conditions that reward it **and** they understand the causal loop the opponent is missing.
